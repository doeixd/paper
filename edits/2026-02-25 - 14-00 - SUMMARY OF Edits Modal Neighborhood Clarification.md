# 2026-02-25 - Modal Neighborhood Clarification

## Rationale

A close reading identified that the relationship between admissible interventions, regimes, and counterfactual possibility space is conceptually present in the paper but never stated in one explicit framing paragraph. The idea that the intervention class defines a modal neighborhood, and that objecthood is stability across that neighborhood, is distributed across multiple sections (2.4, 2.5, 2.9, 4.6). A first-pass reader has to reconstruct this modal architecture; a careful reader can, but shouldn't have to.

## Changes Made

### 1. Added modal-neighborhood framing paragraph in 2.4

**What:** Inserted a short paragraph (4 sentences) in "Admissibility and Hierarchical Evaluation" after the opening paragraph and before the three admissibility constraints. The paragraph states explicitly that the admissible intervention class defines the modal neighborhood across which closure is evaluated, that the regime is partly constituted by that neighborhood, and that objecthood is stability across that space of possibilities.

**Why:** This is the natural home because 2.4 is where the paper first explains that closure is always relative to an intervention class. The new paragraph bridges directly into the three constraints (epistemic, dynamical, explanatory), which then spell out how the modal neighborhood is physically anchored. This makes the paper's modal structure visible on first pass rather than requiring reconstruction.

### 2. Added regime-shift echo in 4.6

**What:** Added one sentence in "Regime Dependence and Projectibility" after "A pattern can be closed in one regime and leaky in another because the structure has changed." The new sentence notes that since the regime is partly defined by the admissible intervention class, a shift in available control channels can change the modal neighborhood and the closure verdict, even when micro-dynamics remain the same.

**Why:** This echoes the 2.4 clarification and makes the regime-dependence section more concrete. It also clarifies an important case: regime change can occur not just because the system's structure changes (ontic regime dependence), but because the available control channels shift (epistemic regime dependence), which changes what can be tested.
