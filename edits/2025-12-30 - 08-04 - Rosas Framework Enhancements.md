## Changes Made

### 1. Substrate Independence Clarification (Line 248)

**Location:** Part 6 (The Nihilist's Retreat), Multiple Realizability section

**Addition:**
> "What makes it 'the same' software is not the physical substrate but the causal structure—the pattern of dependencies between computational states. A causally closed macroscopic process has this same property: it 'runs' regardless of whether its micro-instantiation uses atoms, electrons, or some other substrate, so long as the causal architecture is preserved (Rosas et al. 2024)."

**Rationale:**
- The paper already discussed multiple realizability with the spreadsheet example (silicon, vacuum tubes, abacuses)
- What was missing was the explicit conceptual point: *what* makes something "the same" across substrates
- The addition clarifies that it's the **causal structure** (pattern of computational dependencies), not physical properties
- Connects directly to the computational closure framework
- Strengthens the argument against nihilism by showing that substrate independence implies causal autonomy at the macro level

**Integration:**
- Flows naturally after the existing multiple realizability example
- Avoids repeating the examples (they're already in the text)
- Uses philosophical language consistent with the paper's style
- Properly cites Rosas et al. 2024

### 2. Thermodynamic Efficiency (Line 137)

**Location:** Part 3 (The Birth of Causal Autonomy), Dimensional Reduction section

**Addition:**
> "This reduction is not mere notational convenience. Because information processing costs energy, a system that can predict its future by tracking boundary states alone, rather than every molecule, gains an enormous thermodynamic advantage (Rosas et al. 2024)."

**Rationale:**
- The paper discussed dimensional reduction (high-dimensional → low-dimensional state space)
- What was missing was *why this matters* from a physical/energetic perspective
- The addition provides thermodynamic/evolutionary motivation: compression isn't just mathematical convenience, it's energetically necessary
- Connects to existing mentions of metabolic cost (line 145) and thermodynamic decay (line 109)
- Shows why evolution "discovers" these boundaries: they minimize prediction costs

**Integration:**
- Placed immediately after the dimensional reduction explanation
- Mirrors existing sentence structure ("This is not computational convenience" from line 135)
- Brief and conceptual, avoiding mathematical formalism
- Strengthens the naturalistic case for boundaries being objective discoveries, not arbitrary conventions
