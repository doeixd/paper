## Changes Made

### 1. Enhanced Markov Blanket Explanation (Part 1)

**Location**: Lines 43-53

**What Changed**: Added a castle wall analogy to provide intuitive grounding before the formal probability equation.

**Rationale**: The original text moved quickly from the box example to the mathematical formalism. While the suggestion included "causal event horizon" terminology (which we rejected as unnecessary jargon), the castle wall analogy is an excellent intuitive bridge. It provides a concrete, everyday example that makes the abstract concept of conditional independence immediately graspable: "To predict the temperature inside the great hall, you only need to know the temperature of the stones on the inner surface of the wall. The weather five miles away is irrelevant except insofar as it has already affected those stones."

**Strengthens**: Pedagogical clarity without adding jargon. Holds the reader's hand down the logical path.

### 2. Thermodynamic Grounding of Tolerance (Part 3)

**Location**: Lines 105-109

**What Changed**: Added explicit thermodynamic defense of tolerance parameter before the existing defense of objectivity.

**Rationale**: This addresses what would be a reviewer's sharpest line of attack: "Who decides the error tolerance?" The original response ("interest-relative is not subjective") was correct but could be perceived as evasive. The new text confronts the question directly:

> "The tolerance is not arbitrary; it is the threshold of disintegration. If an organism sets its tolerance too loose (ignoring dangerous external variables), it dies. If it sets it too tight (trying to track every atom), it starves from the computational cost of prediction."

This grounds tolerance in objective survival constraints rather than observer preference. It explicitly shifts the "arbiter" from human judgment to thermodynamics and evolution. This is precisely the kind of pre-emptive defense the paper needs.

**Strengthens**: Closes the subjectivity loophole by anchoring tolerance to physical survival, not preference.

### 3. Multiple Realizability Defense (Part 6)

**Location**: Lines 185-187

**What Changed**: Added explicit discussion of Multiple Realizability with concrete examples (silicon, vacuum tubes, abacuses).

**Rationale**: The paper already discussed supervenience and the software bug analogy, but it didn't explicitly invoke the standard philosophical concept of Multiple Realizability. This addition does three things:

1. **Names the concept**: "Multiple Realizability" is a well-established philosophical term, not introducing unneeded jargon
2. **Provides vivid examples**: The same calculation running on three different substrates makes the point undeniable
3. **Strengthens the causal argument**: "Because the crash can occur on three totally different physical substrates, the *cause* cannot be physical; the cause must be at the algorithmic level."

The hardware engineer microscope analogy drives home why substrate-reduction fails: it's looking at the wrong level of reality.

**Strengthens**: Uses established philosophical terminology to make an airtight case that causes can be structural rather than microphysical.
