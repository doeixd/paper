# The Reality of Patterns

*On reality, instances, representations, and why mathematics is invented—but not freely*

## Abstract

We routinely confuse five different things: reality itself; the patterns reality supports; the concrete instances of those patterns; our mental representations of patterns; and our mental representations of particular instances. These are not the same object, and they fail in different ways. This distinction clarifies what it means for a “whole” (a macro-pattern) to be real, what “causal closure” can and cannot claim, and why mathematics is best understood as *sense-constrained invention*: we create formal systems, but the ones that qualify as “making sense” are filtered by the invariants of intelligibility—stability, compositional inference, coherent consequence—which themselves reflect deep constraints of reality and of agents embedded in it. In this view, higher-level patterns are not optional overlays; they are objectively privileged compressions, selected because they are stable, prediction-preserving consequences of micro-dynamics.

---

## 1. The Five-Layer Ladder

Any serious discussion of “patterns,” “wholes,” “objects,” “numbers,” or “meaning” needs to start by refusing an easy collapse. There are at least five distinct layers:

1. **Reality itself**
   Whatever exists and happens, independent of our models. The ground truth of dynamics.

2. **Patterns supported by reality**
   Regularities, invariants, stable organizations, repeatable causal structures. These are not “in our heads,” though our access to them is mediated.

3. **Instances of patterns**
   Particular tokens: this snowflake, that storm, this chess game, that economic crash. Instances are embedded and messy.

4. **Our representation of patterns**
   Concepts, theories, formal models, learned abstractions. These can be accurate, approximate, or wrong.

5. **Our representation of instances**
   Perception and memory of a token: what you believe happened in this storm, your current board position, your estimate of this firm’s health.

Confusing these layers produces most of the heat in metaphysics. Once separated, several things become obvious:

* A pattern can be real even if our representation of it is crude.
* An instance can fail to match the pattern without making the pattern unreal.
* Our instance-representation can fail even when our pattern-representation is excellent.
* Reality is not obligated to respect our boundaries, even if our boundaries are often useful.

This ladder is not a detour; it is the minimum scaffolding required to talk about “real patterns” without turning the topic into either mysticism (“the pattern is a ghostly object”) or nihilism (“everything is just atoms, so nothing is real”).

---

## 2. Causal Closure Is Type-Level Before It’s Token-Level

The phrase “causal closure” is often heard as an absolute metaphysical stamp: either the macro-system is closed, or it isn’t. That framing is too brittle.

Causal closure is best treated as a property of a *coarse-graining relative to a regime*:

* You choose a macro-description (a pattern).
* You specify what you’re predicting or controlling.
* You specify the relevant horizon and allowable interventions.
* Then you ask: **Does micro-detail add predictive power once we condition on the macro-state?**

Even when the answer is “yes, approximately,” closure is typically a *graded* claim:

* “Closed enough” for the purpose.
* “Leaky” in predictable ways.
* “Fails” under known boundary violations.

Now comes the crucial asymmetry:

> A pattern (type) may achieve causal closure in a regime even when a particular instance (token) does not.

This is not a concession; it is what you should expect from embeddedness.

An instance violates closure when:

* it is hit by external coupling outside the assumed boundary,
* it sits near a phase transition,
* it is driven out of the model’s validity regime,
* or it realizes rare micro-configurations that matter at the macro target.

If you demand token-level perfection, you will deny reality to nearly everything above particle physics. If you allow regime-bounded, purpose-relative closure, you recover the ordinary world without turning it into mere convention.

---

## 3. Two Useful Terms: Entailed Patterns and Stable Patterns

The temptation is to treat emergence as subjective: we “choose” patterns, therefore patterns are “in the head.” The better move is to separate two claims that get bundled under *the pattern is real*: **(i) the pattern is forced by what happened**, and **(ii) the pattern is robust enough to function as an object-level handle**.

These are related but not identical.

Some patterns are real as structures (closed consequence relations), whether or not they are currently instantiated. Other patterns are real as implementations (stable macro-dynamics in physical substrates). The paper’s causal-closure criterion is primarily a test for implementation-realness; structure-realness is instead given by definability and internal closure.

### 3.1 Entailed Pattern

An **entailed pattern** is a macro-regularity that is *forced by the actual microhistory*.

Fix:

* the laws,
* the actual microstate,
* and therefore the actual trajectory.

Then certain macro-features are not “interpretations.” They are consequences. They are literally *in the facts* of the worldline. If the microdynamics produced dendritic ice growth, or a hurricane eye-wall, or an industrial supply chain, those are not optional overlays. They are baked into what happened.

Two clarifications matter:

* This is a **tenseless** claim about the worldline, not a claim about what is currently instantiated.
* It does **not** require alternate initial conditions. You don’t need to say “it could have been otherwise” to say “given what did occur, this macro-regularity is a real feature of the trajectory.”

Entailed patterns are the antidote to the idea that macro-structure is “just a story we tell.” Some stories are simply *forced* by the microfacts.

### 3.2 Stable Pattern

A **stable pattern** is an entailed pattern with an additional property: it is *robust under coarse-graining and perturbations (within a regime)*. Stability is what turns “something that happened” into “something with objecthood.”

A stable pattern is one where:

* many microstates map to the same macrostate,
* the macrostate is predictively sufficient for macro-futures (micro-detail mostly cancels out at the macro target),
* and the macro-dynamics persist under small shocks and boundary noise.

Stability is where **causal closure** lives. Closure is rarely perfect, but stability gives you the right kind of imperfection: bounded leakiness rather than arbitrary sensitivity.

### 3.3 Relationship: Every Stable Pattern Is Entailed, But Not Vice Versa

This is the key refinement:

* **Stable patterns are also entailed patterns**—because if a stable macro-regularity holds, it holds as a fact about the actual trajectory.
* But **not every entailed pattern is stable**. Some macro-features are true of what happened but are brittle, non-repeatable, or hyper-sensitive to microdetail. They can be entailed without earning objecthood.

So the distinction is not “real vs unreal.” It is:

* **Entailed** = “this macro-regularity is genuinely in the worldline.”
* **Stable** = “this macro-regularity is robust enough to be a privileged compression—an objective handle for prediction and control.”

Entailed patterns give you **non-arbitrariness**. Stable patterns give you **objecthood**.

## 4. Snowflakes: Emergence Without Mysticism

Snowflakes are a perfect demonstration because they are physically grounded and intuitively familiar.

Suppose you had a complete microdescription of the conditions surrounding a snowflake’s formation: temperature gradients, humidity, airflow, impurities, the nucleation site, molecular interactions, the lot. Given laws, the resulting structure is fixed.

From that, patterns emerge necessarily:

* hexagonal symmetry at certain scales,
* branching, faceting, dendritic motifs,
* correlations between macroscopic “style” and growth conditions.

Importantly, the inevitability is not the inevitability of an exact pixel-level geometry. Fine detail can be extremely sensitive. The inevitability is the inevitability of *macro-regularities* that are selected by constraints.

What do we learn?

1. **Patterns are not optional overlays**
   The macro-structure is not a story you tell about molecules. It is a lawful consequence of molecular dynamics under constraints.

2. **Token-level detail is not the standard for reality**
   The exact micro-branch is not stable; the growth mode is.

3. **Compression can be objectively privileged**
   The right macro-variables are not chosen arbitrarily. They are the ones that preserve prediction at lower cost than tracking everything.

This is the template. Once you accept it for snowflakes, you can accept it for many higher-level wholes—provided you do not demand that every whole be as clean as a crystal.

## 5. Formal Systems Are Invented, But Not Freely

Now shift from snowflakes to mathematics.

We invent notations, axioms, proof rules. That’s human work. But it does not follow that mathematics is “arbitrary,” as though any pile of squiggles and inference rules is equally eligible for the title. Just as with chess and English, we need the same separation:

* **a definable structure** (the constraint system itself),
* **an implementation** (something that runs or carries it),
* **a representation** (an internal model of it in a mind).

Mathematics lives primarily at the first layer.

### Mathematics as substrate-independent constraint structure

A formal system—stripped of typography—is a **self-contained relational constraint pattern**: a set of primitive relations plus rules that fix what follows from what. In that sense, it is like chess-as-structure: **closed by definition**. Given the axioms and inference rules, the space of valid derivations is determined internally. No external “physics” is required to determine whether a proof is valid; validity is an internal property of the structure.

This is what people sometimes dismiss as “if-then-ism,” but that dismissal misses the point. “If-then” is not a downgrade; it is a statement of structure:

> If these relations hold, then these relations follow.

That is exactly what it means to specify a constrained dynamical or relational system. In that respect, a formal system is not a heap of symbols—it is a description of a possible structure. And describing possible structure is not ontologically alien to describing the universe; the universe is itself a vast constraint network in which some states follow from others.

### Invented, but not freely: the invariants of intelligibility

Here is the key point:

> Mathematics is invented—but not freely. What we can invent and still regard as *making sense* is constrained by the invariants of intelligibility.

Those invariants include:

* **stable identity / reidentification** (we can track “the same thing” across transformations),
* **compositional inference** (consequences can be built by composing relations),
* **coherent consequence** (rules don’t collapse into triviality),
* **structure-preserving transformation** (symmetries and invariants are meaningful and conserved under the right maps).

These are not merely social conventions. They are the operating requirements for any agent embedded in a structured world to successfully track, predict, and intervene. They are, in effect, the cognitive shadow of reality’s constraints.

So when someone says, “You’re welcome to create a mathematics that doesn’t mirror reality,” the natural response is: “But that wouldn’t make sense.”

That response is not prejudice. It is recognition that **sense itself is reality-shaped**. The space of coherent formal systems is not an unconstrained playground; it is a filtered space whose filters are set by what intelligibility requires.

### Implementation and representation

A formal system can exist as a definable structure without any current implementation. But to do mathematics in practice, it must be **implemented** somewhere (ink, silicon, neural activity) and **represented** by some agent (a cognitive model of the rules and objects). Those are separate layers again:

* the structure is substrate-independent,
* the implementation is physical,
* the representation is psychological and fallible.

The structure does not depend on our minds to be what it is; our access to it does.

## 6. Numbers as Patterns Supported by Reality

Numbers, in this view, are not magic objects floating in a void. They are **stable constraint patterns** that become objectively privileged because reality—and agents embedded in it—recurrently instantiate the conditions that make them useful.

What is “two-ness”? Not a stamp on the universe, but an invariant of systems that support:

* distinguishable individuals,
* grouping and separation,
* stable reidentification across time,
* conservation under counting-like operations.

If the world supports stable individuation and stable combination, then arithmetic is not a random convention. It is an **objectively privileged compression**—the simplest stable way to track repeated structure across many domains.

Here again the same ladder applies:

* **Number as structure:** the relations fixed by arithmetic axioms (substrate-independent).
* **Number as implemented:** concrete counting, measurement, computation in physical systems.
* **Number as represented:** a cognitive model of quantity in a mind.

The reason “pure” mathematics keeps latching onto the world is not mystical coincidence. It is that mathematics explores the space of intelligible structures, and reality—under constraints—recurrently instantiates many of the same invariants (symmetry, composition, conservation, order).

---

## 7. Logic: Reality-Shaped Filters on Consequence

Consider excluded middle, non-contradiction (in many regimes), causality-like ordering, and related principles.

On this view, logical rules are not primarily arbitrary stipulations. They are:

* **patterns we observe** in stable reasoning about a structured world,
* **constraints** that successful tracking agents must satisfy to preserve coherence,
* and therefore reflections—imperfect but real—of deep regularities in how consequence works for beings like us in a world like this.

This does not mean every logical principle is universal at every scale or in every domain. It means:

> The logics we treat as “making sense” are filtered by what coherent tracking in this kind of reality demands.

Different domains can demand different filters. When you shift regimes—constructive mathematics, inconsistent information systems, quantum measurement contexts—the “right” logic can shift with it. That pluralism does not undermine the core claim. It illustrates it: **different constraint regimes select different invariants of intelligibility**, and logic is our attempt to write those invariants down.

## 8.1 Chess

Start with chess, because it makes the separation vivid.

There are two different senses in which chess is “closed,” and mixing them up creates most of the confusion.

### Chess as a substrate-independent, causally closed rule-structure

Chess, at its core, is a **fully specified relational dynamics**: a state space (board positions plus side-to-move and castling/en-passant rights) and a transition rule (“legal move” as a constraint on allowed state updates), plus terminal conditions (checkmate, stalemate, etc.). In that sense, chess is **causally closed by definition**: given a chess-state, the set of legal successors is determined entirely within the chess description. Nothing outside the rule-structure is required to define what follows from what.

This is not “in your head,” and it is not “in the wood.” It is a pattern of relations—a self-contained constraint system. It is **substrate-independent** in the precise sense that it can be realized in wood, silicon, ink, or pure description without changing what chess *is*. You do not need an implementation in order for the structure to be what it is.

On the paper’s terms, this is chess as an **entailed pattern**: once reality contains agents who can specify relational systems at all, the existence of such systems is not mysterious. A game like chess is a real feature of the world’s structure-space—something that can be stated, reasoned about, and whose internal consequences are fixed. Its reality here is the reality of a constraint pattern: *if these relations, then these consequences*.

### Chess as an implementation (a running instance)

Separate from that is chess as something that **runs**—an actual game being played, a computer executing move generation, a tournament enforcing legality. That requires a substrate to carry out the updates. It is chess as an **implemented pattern**: a physical process whose macrostate is interpretable as a chess position and whose transitions track the chess transition rule.

In that implemented sense, chess is causally active only where the universe is actually executing it—where some substrate is doing the updating, enforcing legality, and propagating the state forward.

### If Earth were destroyed

So if all humans died and Earth were destroyed—no players, no books, no computers, no stored descriptions—then:

* **Chess would still be a causally closed, substrate-independent structure.** Its rule-closure does not depend on being instantiated.
* **But there would be no implementations of chess anywhere in our local universe.** No running games, no physical encodings, no artifacts that carry the state forward under chess constraints.

The difference is the difference between **a lawlike relation** and **a process that instantiates it**. Chess-as-structure remains what it is; chess-as-running-dynamics disappears when all substrates that encode or execute it are gone.

And separately: your concept of chess—the **represented pattern**—is a third thing again. It is an implementation too, but an implementation **in neural substrate**: a cognitive model that can be accurate or mistaken, partial or complete. It is neither the abstract rule-structure itself nor a particular played game; it is your internal compression of that structure.

## 8.2 English

English has the same three-way separation as chess—**rule-structure**, **implementation**, **representation**—but the balance shifts. Where chess is a deliberately closed transition system, English is a **living coordination pattern** whose closure is weaker and whose substrate requirements are heavier.

### English as a substrate-independent structure of constraints

At one level, English can be treated as a **definable relational system**: a lexicon, grammatical constraints, compositional rules, and (at least in principle) semantics as rules for constructing meanings from parts. In that “structural” sense, there is something real and determinate to talk about even when nobody is currently speaking. You can specify the constraints; you can derive consequences (“this sentence is grammatical,” “this parse is licensed,” “this entailment follows given these meanings”).

That’s English-as-structure: not ink, not sound waves, not neurons, but a pattern of relations that can be stated. It is substrate-independent in the same minimal sense as chess: it can be encoded in books, memories, or machines without changing what the constraints are.

But unlike chess, English is not fully causally closed *as a structure* in the same clean way. The “update rule” is not a compact set of mechanical transitions. Meaning is context-sensitive; usage evolves; boundaries are negotiated. So the structure exists, but it is a **softer closure**: a constraint system with gray zones, not a total transition function.

### English as an implemented pattern (a running coordination process)

English becomes a *worldly*, causally active pattern when it is implemented as a **community-level dynamical process**:

* production and perception of utterances,
* shared conventions about reference and intent,
* correction and norm enforcement,
* transmission across time,
* continual adaptation to new contexts.

Here, “meaning” is not ink. It is a stable mapping maintained by an ongoing interpretive system. The causal powers of English—its ability to coordinate action, transmit plans, create institutions, bind commitments—live in this implemented layer.

So if humans die and no other interpreters exist:

* books remain as **marks**,
* recordings remain as **signals**,
* but English does not remain as a *running*, causally efficacious coordination pattern—because the substrate that enacts interpretation, stabilization, and norm enforcement is gone.

The crucial point is that the marks can still *encode* the structure, but without a system that can decode and act on it, the pattern is no longer operative in the causal fabric of the world.

### English as represented pattern (the internal model)

Again, your internal model of English is a third thing: English-as-represented. It is itself an implementation—in neural substrate—of (some approximation to) the structural constraints and meanings. It can be incomplete, dialect-bound, mistaken, or idiosyncratic. But it is still a real pattern, just not the same pattern as English-as-community-practice.

So English exists in at least three ways:

* as **structure** (constraints you can state),
* as **implementation** (a running social coordination dynamic),
* as **representation** (a model in a mind).

These can come apart.

## 8.3 The General Point

This yields a broader classification that avoids both reductionism and mysticism.

### Self-implementing patterns

Some patterns are **self-implementing under physics alone**: crystals, hurricanes, orbital resonances. Their “implementation” is just ordinary dynamics of matter and energy. No additional norm-enforcing substrate is required for the pattern to run.

### Constraint-defined patterns with implementation requirements

Other patterns are **constraint-defined but implementation-dependent** for their causal role: languages, laws, games, institutions. Their structures can be specified substrate-independently, but their *worldly existence as active patterns* depends on substrates that enforce norms, stabilize meanings, adjudicate legality, and carry state forward.

This dependence does not demote them to unreality. It identifies their ontological conditions—what must be present for them to become stable compressions that do causal work.

Or more tersely:

* A **definable constraint pattern** can exist without any current instance running it.
* A **represented pattern** can exist in a mind even when it mis-tracks the external structure.
* An **implemented pattern** exists only where the world is actually executing it—where some substrate is maintaining the constraints over time.

## 9. Determinism, Necessity, and “What Could Have Been”

A common move is to say: “If the universe is deterministic, then chess was always going to exist, therefore chess exists necessarily.”

The correct version is more precise:

* Given a complete microdescription of the actual world + laws, the actual history is fixed.
* If chess occurs in that history, then chess is **entailed** by that total constraint structure.

That is a strong claim. But it is not the same as saying chess is a timelessly instantiated object.

Entailed patterns are features of the worldline. Stable patterns are features of regimes where they are implemented and robust.

You do not need to take a stand on whether “different initial conditions” are metaphysically possible in order to make the paper’s core point. You can say:

* In the actual history, some macro-patterns are forced consequences of micro-dynamics.
* Their “reality” is grounded in their explanatory and causal role when instantiated.
* And our freedom is not in deciding what is real, but in choosing which stable compressions we track.

---

## 10. The Central Thesis

We can now state the thesis cleanly:

1. Reality supports patterns.
2. Some patterns are objectively privileged compressions because they are stable, prediction-preserving consequences of micro-dynamics.
3. Instances may violate closure even when the pattern is closed in a regime.
4. Our representations are neither the pattern nor the instance; they are fallible compressions shaped by the same constraints that shape the world.
5. Mathematics and logic are invented, but not freely: “making sense” is filtered by invariants of intelligibility that reflect deep constraints of reality and of embedded agents.

So higher-level patterns are not mere overlays. They are not “only in the head.” They are features of the world that become visible when you respect the ladder and use causal closure as a regime-bounded, graded criterion rather than an absolutist stamp.

---

## 11. Objections and Replies

### Objection 1: “This makes reality epistemic: it’s just what predicts.”

Reply: Prediction is diagnostic, not constitutive. The point is not “whatever predicts exists.” The point is that **stable causal organization** is what prediction tracks. The compression is privileged because the world’s dynamics make it privileged.

### Objection 2: “If patterns depend on agents, they’re not real.”

Reply: Dependence on an implementation substrate is not unreality. A hurricane depends on an atmosphere. A flame depends on fuel and oxygen. A language depends on agents and transmission. The substrate conditions differ; the metaphysical pattern-reality is the same kind of claim.

### Objection 3: “Math is arbitrary; you can always change axioms.”

Reply: You can change axioms, but not all changes preserve intelligibility. The space of “systems we can regard as making sense” is filtered by stability, compositional inference, and coherent consequence. That filter is not arbitrary; it reflects what reality demands of tracking agents.

---

## Conclusion

The world is not a list of atoms plus a hallucination layer of wholes. Nor is it a Platonic catalog of perfect objects. It is structured dynamics under constraints.

Patterns are real insofar as they are stable compressions the world itself supports—entailed by micro-dynamics and, in favorable regimes, causally closed at the macro level. Instances can be messy without dissolving the pattern. Our representations can be wrong without making the pattern subjective.

Mathematics is the disciplined exploration of intelligible structure. It is invented, but not freely, because intelligibility is reality-shaped. That is why mathematics “mirrors” the universe: not by coincidence, and not because symbols are magic, but because both are constrained relational systems—and the constraints select the same invariants.
