# Cited Source Summaries for *Real Patterns Need Closure*

This document summarizes each source currently cited in the Synthese manuscript and explains how it directly supports the paper's philosophical argument.

## 1) Ainsworth 2009 (`ainsworth2009`)

Source: Peter M. Ainsworth, "Newman's Objection," *The British Journal for the Philosophy of Science* 60(1): 135-171 (2009). ([text version](<text-converted/Newmans Objection - The British Journal for the Philosophy of Science 2009-03_  -  ISSN 0007-0882, Volume 60, Issue 1, 2009-03  -  Oxford Publishing Limited(England)  -  00070882  -.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Ainsworth offers a systematic reconstruction of Newman-style objections to structural realism, especially in Ramsey-sentence formulations. The core pressure is that once theories are reformulated in sufficiently extensional structural terms, realist commitments can collapse into thin existence-and-cardinality claims. The paper surveys major replies and argues that they do not fully block trivialization. It also shows that shifting formulations does not by itself remove the underlying worry.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): This source motivates the paper's insistence that compression or structural fit alone cannot ground ontology. The manuscript uses closure as an anti-triviality filter that requires transition and intervention structure to be preserved, not merely redescribed. It also helps frame a reviewer-facing caution: empirical fit can still be ontologically underdetermining.

Caution: Use Ainsworth as a constraint on permissive structure-only realism, not as a global refutation of every structural realist position.

## 2) Ando and Simon 1961 (`ando1961`)

Source: Albert Ando and Herbert A. Simon, "Aggregation of Variables in Dynamic Systems," *Econometrica* 29(2): 111-138 (1961). ([text version](<text-converted/Ando A Simon Aggragation of variables in dynamic systems - Econometrica  1961-04_ Vol 29 Iss 2  -  ISSN 0012-9682, Volume 29, Issue 2, 1961-04  -  Blackwell Publishing Ltd_  -  00129682.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Ando and Simon formalize near decomposability in dynamic systems, where strong within-subsystem interactions coexist with weaker cross-subsystem couplings. They show how this structure yields temporal separation: short-run local dynamics and longer-run aggregate regularities. Their matrix analysis gives principled conditions under which aggregation remains informative instead of arbitrary. The result is an early and rigorous account of when coarse-grained dynamics are defensible.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses this as technical precedent for approximate closure and regime-indexed macro realism. It supports the claim that macro-level regularities can be objective under specific coupling and timescale conditions, even without exact closure. It also clarifies that defensible aggregation depends on structure, not convenience.

Caution: Ando-Simon provides mathematical conditions for aggregation quality, not an independent metaphysical criterion.

## 3) Cover and Thomas 2006 (`cover2006`)

Source: Thomas M. Cover and Joy A. Thomas, *Elements of Information Theory*, 2nd ed. (2006). ([text version](<text-converted/Elements of Information Theory -- Thomas M_ Cover & Joy A_ T__9093e12894.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Cover and Thomas provides the standard framework for entropy, mutual information, conditional mutual information, coding limits, and data-processing constraints. The text is not only definitional. It ties each quantity to operational meanings about compression, transmission, and inferential loss. This makes informational dependence and residual dependence formally tractable across model transformations.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript's leakiness and closure diagnostics rely on these definitions, especially conditional mutual information for residual micro-to-macro predictive contribution given the current macrostate. This lets the paper state closure failure in a mathematically disciplined way. It also prevents purely metaphorical use of "information."

Caution: Information theory supplies formal constraints, but it does not by itself settle ontological commitment.

## 4) Dennett 1991 (`dennett1991`)

Source: Daniel C. Dennett, "Real Patterns," *The Journal of Philosophy* 88(1): 27-51 (1991). ([text version](<text-converted/Dennett_RealPatterns.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Dennett defends a realist stance toward patterns that produce genuine compressive and predictive payoff. His criterion is comparative: if a higher-level description tracks robust regularities better than brute enumeration, it earns ontological seriousness. He explicitly rejects trivial naming tricks that mimic structure without explanatory traction. The paper is a middle path between eliminativism and naive realism.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): This is the manuscript's starting point. It adopts Dennett's compression insight, then argues that closure is additionally required to rule out transition-incoherent or intervention-fragile macro groupings. The paper is therefore an extension and tightening of the real-patterns framework.

Caution: Do not present Dennett as already giving the manuscript's closure criterion.

## 5) Elgin 2017 (`elgin2017`)

Source: Catherine Z. Elgin, *True Enough* (2017). ([text version](<text-converted/True Enough.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Elgin argues that epistemic success is not exhausted by strict literal truth because science often uses idealizations and controlled distortions to achieve understanding. Her account of felicitous falsehood explains how inexact models can still be norm-governed and cognitively valuable. The broader lesson is that understanding can be advanced by disciplined representational practice, not only by pointwise truth.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses Elgin to mark the gap between descriptive utility and ontological warrant. This supports the move from usefulness alone to closure-constrained admissibility. It also helps pre-empt objections that any idealized macro model must therefore be merely instrumental.

Caution: Elgin refines epistemic norms; she does not independently defend closure realism.

## 6) Fodor 1974 (`fodor1974`)

Source: Jerry A. Fodor, "Special Sciences (or: The Disunity of Science as a Working Hypothesis)," *Synthese* 28(2): 97-115 (1974). ([text version](<text-converted/Special sciences or The disunity of science as a working hypothesis J A Fodor.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Fodor argues that higher-level sciences can retain explanatory autonomy under multiple realization without requiring strict type-level reduction to physics. He separates token physicalism from stronger reductionist demands and shows why bridge-law identity can fail while special-science generalizations remain explanatory. The paper gives a durable argument for level-sensitive scientific practice under physical constraint.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses Fodor to motivate autonomy pressure, then adds that autonomy alone is insufficient for ontology. Closure provides the additional discipline needed to separate robust macro kinds from permissive regroupings. Multiple realization is treated as a background condition, not a sufficient test.

Caution: Do not infer from Fodor that any higher-level grouping is ontologically admissible.

## 7) Gladziejewski 2025 (`gladziejewski2025`)

Source: Pawel Gladziejewski, "Real patterns, the predictive mind, and the cognitive construction of the manifest image," *Synthese* 206:225 (2025). ([text version](<text-converted/Real patterns, the predictive mind.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Gladziejewski links Dennettian pattern realism to predictive-processing accounts of cognition. The paper argues that bounded cognizers converge on compressive, prediction-serving macro representations and that this convergence can be philosophically informative. It integrates real-patterns talk with contemporary cognitive architecture, while preserving the distinction between representational uptake and metaphysical commitment.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses this as a convergence genealogy for why certain macro descriptions are cognitively stable. It then argues that convergence by itself is still not enough for ontology. Closure is introduced as the additional filter that distinguishes uptake from admissibility.

Caution: Avoid treating cognitive convergence as equivalent to ontological warrant.

## 8) Goodman 1955/1983 (`goodman1955`)

Source: Nelson Goodman, *Fact, Fiction, and Forecast* (orig. 1955; 4th ed. 1983). ([text version](<text-converted/goodman_fact_fiction_and_forecast.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Goodman reframes induction as a projectibility problem rather than a purely extensional fit problem. Through grue-style constructions, he shows that past fit alone underdetermines future projection unless predicate use is constrained. His view emphasizes disciplined mutual adjustment among inferential rules, classificatory practice, and judgment.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses Goodman to motivate admissibility and iteration logic in partition and regime choice. Closure constraints function as part of this discipline by ruling out merely extensional but dynamically unstable regroupings. The result is anti-foundational without sliding into unrestricted coherence.

Caution: Goodman constrains projection practice, but he does not license relativist permissiveness.

## 9) Haugeland 1998 (`haugeland1998`)

Source: John Haugeland, *Having Thought: Essays in the Metaphysics of Mind* (1998). ([text version](<text-converted/Having thought _ essays in the metaphysics of mind -- John H__4ee8f9ff79.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Haugeland argues that representational success must answer to robust standards of correctness, not mere practical convenience. In his engagement with pattern-oriented views, he stresses that objective accountability and genuine error-possibility are essential to realism. This pushes beyond a purely instrumental reading of successful representation.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript cites Haugeland where it distinguishes pattern utility from ontological warrant. Closure is presented as the additional criterion that secures accountable macro commitments by testing structural stability under transition and intervention. Haugeland supplies the normative pressure point; the manuscript adds formal machinery.

Caution: Haugeland motivates the problem but does not provide the manuscript's closure diagnostics.

## 10) Hoel, Albantakis, and Tononi 2013 (`hoel2013`)

Source: Erik P. Hoel, Larissa Albantakis, and Giulio Tononi, "Quantifying causal emergence shows that macro can beat micro," *PNAS* 110(49): 19790-19795 (2013). ([text version](<text-converted/Quantifying causal emergence shows that macro can beat micro Erik P Hoel, Larissa Albantakis, Giulio Tononi.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Hoel and collaborators define effective-information-based causal emergence and show conditions where macro models can outperform micro models on causal informativeness. Their framework identifies when coarse-graining can reduce degeneracy and noise enough to improve intervention-relevant structure. The paper demonstrates that macro superiority can be principled rather than merely heuristic.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript treats EI gains as evidence that macro descriptions can be causally significant under explicit criteria. It then argues this is still not a standalone ontological test, because informational advantage can coexist with problematic partitions. Closure remains the decisive discriminant.

Caution: Avoid "macro always beats micro" formulations.

## 11) Kemeny and Snell 1960 (`kemeny1960`)

Source: John G. Kemeny and J. Laurie Snell, *Finite Markov Chains* (1960). ([text version](<text-converted/Finite Markov Chains.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Kemeny and Snell provide classical finite-chain theory, including criteria for valid state aggregation and lumpability. Their key contribution for this project is explicit conditions under which coarse-graining preserves Markovian transition structure. They also distinguish stronger and weaker aggregation conditions, clarifying that preservation quality can vary by setup and distributional assumptions.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): This is the manuscript's exact benchmark for ideal closure. The argument then extends from strict lumpability to graded diagnostics for non-ideal empirical settings. Kemeny-Snell therefore anchors the formal side of the closure framework.

Caution: Lumpability is a mathematical property, not a complete metaphysical theory.

## 12) Kim 1998 (`kim1998`)

Source: Jaegwon Kim, *Mind in a Physical World* (1998). ([text version](<text-converted/Mind in a Physical World_ An Essay on the Mind-Body Problem__15c7e1de45.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Kim develops exclusion pressure against nonreductive accounts of higher-level causal efficacy under physical closure. His argument is that if microphysical causes are already sufficient, macro causes risk redundancy unless dependence is clarified in a non-inflationary way. The book makes this challenge central to debates about mental causation and supervenience.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses Kim as the strongest challenge to macro realism and causal autonomy claims. Its response is target-indexed non-redundancy under closure, rather than dual-cause inflation. Kim's pressure defines the burden the closure view must meet.

Caution: Do not present Kim as denying all macro explanation.

## 13) Kim 2005 (`kim2005`)

Source: Jaegwon Kim, *Physicalism, or Something Near Enough* (2005). ([text version](<text-converted/Physicalism or Something Near Enough -- Kim, Jaegwon(Author)__e679cebe4a.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Kim extends exclusion arguments and reassesses whether nonreductive physicalist packages can preserve irreducible yet causally potent higher-level properties. He sharpens the instability claim for many standard reconciliations and argues for a more constrained physicalist position. The work intensifies the demand for explicit non-redundancy conditions.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses this to reinforce the exclusion challenge. Closure is offered as a way to show when macro variables remain interventionally and predictively non-redundant despite micro implementation. This supports selective, criterion-based macro commitment instead of blanket autonomy claims.

Caution: The manuscript responds to Kim's challenge; it does not show Kim endorses closure realism.

## 14) Ladyman and Ross 2007 (`ladyman2007`)

Source: James Ladyman and Don Ross, *Every Thing Must Go: Metaphysics Naturalized* (2007). ([text version](<text-converted/Every Thing Must Go.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Ladyman and Ross defend naturalized metaphysics and structure-first realism constrained by scientific practice. They reject unconstrained armchair ontology and emphasize projectibility, robustness, and anti-gerrymandering standards for metaphysical commitment. Their account strengthens the idea that ontological claims should track disciplined explanatory structure.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript inherits this naturalized and anti-gerrymandering orientation. It then introduces closure as a stricter, more explicit discriminant for macro objecthood. This positions the project as continuous with ETMG while adding operational tightening.

Caution: Do not attribute the paper's specific closure protocol directly to ETMG.

## 15) Lewis 1986 (`lewis1986`)

Source: David Lewis, *On the Plurality of Worlds* (1986). ([text version](<text-converted/Lewis-David-(1986)-On-the-Plurality-of-Worlds.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Lewis defends permissive composition in a broader modal-metaphysical framework, including highly counterintuitive fusions. A central lesson for this project is that existence claims can be much cheaper than ordinary classificatory seriousness. This creates a sharp distinction between what exists and what is ontologically significant for explanatory practice.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses Lewis as a permissiveness baseline to separate bare existence from robust macro-object realism. Closure explains why structured entities such as hurricanes can differ from gerrymandered fusions in ontological status for this project. The move is not anti-Lewis. It adds a criterion for selective macro commitment.

Caution: Lewis provides permissiveness pressure, not a closure criterion.

## 16) Pearl 2009 (keyed as `pearl2000`)

Source: Judea Pearl, *Causality: Models, Reasoning, and Inference*, 2nd ed. (2009; key retained as `pearl2000`). ([text version](<text-converted/Pearl_2009_Causality.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Pearl develops the structural causal model framework, the `do`-operator, and a principled distinction between observation and intervention. This provides explicit semantics for intervention, invariance assumptions, and causal identification under model constraints. The framework turns causal discourse into disciplined model-based inference.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript draws on Pearl for intervention semantics and non-correlational causal interpretation. It then adds admissibility constraints so merely formal intervention variables do not automatically ground ontology claims. Closure is layered on top of interventionism as an ontological filter.

Caution: Pearl's framework does not itself determine macro objecthood criteria.

## 17) Putnam 1967 (`putnam1967`)

Source: Hilary Putnam, "Psychological Predicates," in *Art, Mind, and Religion* (1967). ([text version](<text-converted/3027PutnamPsychPredicates.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Putnam's early functionalism argues against strict type-identity by individuating psychological kinds via causal-functional role rather than substrate identity. The multiple-realization point is central. Distinct physical realizers can instantiate the same psychological predicate if they satisfy the relevant role profile. This blocks simple reductions from higher-level kindhood to one physical kind.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses Putnam to support anti-reductionist background commitments while preserving physical implementation. It then argues that realization diversity still does not by itself warrant macro ontology. Closure is introduced as the additional test for transition and intervention coherence.

Caution: Do not treat Putnam's functionalism as a complete macro ontology criterion.

Core contribution (detailed): Rosas et al. formalize hierarchical emergence using computational-mechanics tools, including explicit closure-style diagnostics. The framework relates predictive sufficiency, causal relevance, and coarse-graining quality through machine-based representations. Direct relation to the paper (detailed): This provides major formal machinery for the manuscript's predictive closure diagnostics. The manuscript then adds philosophical commitment protocols, including admissibility constraints, robustness checks, and downgrade rules. Rosas et al. gives the technical base, while the paper strengthens the ontological decision layer.

Caution: Do not imply Rosas et al. already contains the manuscript's full ontological criterion.

## 23) Woodward 2015 (`woodward2015`)

Source: James Woodward, "Methodology, Ontology, and Interventionism," *Synthese* 192(11): 3577-3599 (2015). ([text version](<text-converted/Methodology, Ontology, and Interventionism.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Woodward emphasizes interventionism as a methodological framework for causal inquiry rather than a heavy standalone metaphysical doctrine. He distinguishes practical ontology-building in science from stronger grounding-focused metaphysical ambitions. This keeps causal objectivity tied to disciplined variable choice, intervention semantics, and inferential stability.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses this to support a method-first strategy. Ontology claims are earned through constrained interventional practice and stability diagnostics, not asserted independently. Closure is presented as a further criterion within that methodological spirit.

Caution: Methodological priority is not equivalent to anti-realism.

## 24) Woodward 2021 (`woodward2021`)

Source: James Woodward, *Causation with a Human Face: Normative Theory and Descriptive Psychology* (2021). ([text version](<text-converted/Causation with a Human Face_ Normative Theory and -- James Francis Woodward -- Oxford Studies.txt>))

Core contribution (detailed): Woodward connects interventionist theory with variable-selection norms, including graded invariance and proportionality. He argues for non-binary evaluation of causal representations, where some variable choices are better calibrated to explanatory and interventional goals than others. The account integrates normative and descriptive dimensions without collapsing one into the other.

Direct relation to the paper (detailed): The manuscript uses this to support graded evaluation and thresholded commitment levels, aligning with qualified-versus-robust closure distinctions. It helps justify why commitment can be tiered instead of all-or-nothing. Closure remains stricter than proportionality, but conceptually adjacent in discipline.

Caution: Proportionality in Woodward is a graded virtue and should not be treated as identical to the manuscript's closure test.
