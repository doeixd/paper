# dewhurst2020causal

## Full Citation
Joe Dewhurst, "Causal Emergence and Real Patterns" (preprint, April 2020).

## Detailed Summary
This preprint evaluates Erik Hoel's effective information (EI) framework as an account of causal emergence. Dewhurst reconstructs the central claim in Hoel's work: some coarse-grainings increase EI relative to micro-descriptions, and this seems to support the idea that macro-level interventions can be more causally informative than micro-level interventions.

Dewhurst grants the formal result but contests its metaphysical interpretation. He argues that the causal notion at work is interventionist in a relatively deflationary sense, so the framework primarily tracks explanatory informativeness, not robustly novel causal powers. On this reading, EI can show that some macro-descriptions are better for prediction and intervention-guidance, while still leaving traditional strong-emergence ambitions unfulfilled.

A central pressure point is fat-handedness. If macro-level interventions necessarily involve changes in the microbase, Dewhurst argues, then it is hard to infer autonomous macro-causal powers from improved macro-level intervention profiles alone. He also situates the argument against Kim-style exclusion concerns, suggesting Hoel's framework does not yet underwrite a strong anti-reductionist conclusion.

The paper's constructive move is to reinterpret Hoel's results through a Dennett-Ladyman-Ross real-patterns lens. Dewhurst's positive claim is that EI results are best treated as showing epistemically valuable and projectible patterns. They identify when a macro-description captures useful and stable structure for inquiry, but do not by themselves force a strong ontological emergence thesis.

The conclusion is explicitly calibrated: Hoel's framework is philosophically useful and formally illuminating, but supports weak or epistemic emergence unless one adopts additional commitments about causation and ontology.

## Core Argument Map
- **Setup:** Hoel's EI framework can produce macro-level informational advantages under coarse-graining.
- **Interpretive question:** Do those advantages establish genuinely novel macro-causal powers?
- **Critical claim:** EI tracks interventionist explanatory strength, not necessarily strong causal power.
- **Objection channel:** Fat-handedness and exclusion worries block a direct move from EI gains to robust ontological emergence.
- **Reconstruction:** EI results fit best as real-pattern style epistemic emergence.
- **Conclusion:** The framework is significant, but metaphysically weaker than many causal-emergence proponents suggest.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure
This source is a direct foil for closure-based realism. Dewhurst accepts the informational achievements of macro-level models but argues that those achievements remain epistemic. A closure criterion can be read as a response strategy: it tries to supply additional constraints on admissibility, transition autonomy, and robustness that Dewhurst takes to be missing from EI-only arguments.

For RPNC, the strongest use is dialectical and defensive. Dewhurst helps formulate the reviewer objection that information-theoretic gains alone do not secure macro-objecthood. Citing him can sharpen the paper's burden: show why closure plus admissibility is meant to block the deflationary interpretation, rather than merely restate EI benefits.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions
- It is a preprint and may differ in detail from the later journal version.
- Dewhurst does not deny the formal EI result; he disputes its metaphysical force.
- The critique targets strong emergence claims specifically, not the practical utility of coarse-grained models.
- Fat-handedness remains a live methodological objection in intervention-based emergence debates.
