# dewhurst2021causal

## Full Citation
Joe Dewhurst, "Causal emergence from effective information: Neither causal nor emergent?," *Thought: A Journal of Philosophy* 10, no. 3 (2021): 158-168. https://doi.org/10.1002/tht3.489.

## Detailed Summary
This journal article is the peer-reviewed development of Dewhurst's critique of information-theoretic causal emergence. Dewhurst examines Hoel's effective information program and argues that, although the formal framework is interesting and potentially useful, it does not by itself establish robust metaphysical emergence.

The first stage of the argument reconstructs EI as a measure of intervention-guided informativeness across coarse-grained descriptions. Dewhurst accepts that macro-level descriptions can outperform micro-level descriptions under this metric. He emphasizes that this is a real and non-trivial finding about explanatory structure.

The second stage asks whether the result is genuinely causal in the stronger sense often assumed in emergence debates. Dewhurst argues that the causation at issue is interventionist and methodologically thin. On this view, EI tracks which manipulations are more informative for prediction and control, not whether new causal powers have appeared at the macro-level.

The third stage addresses emergence. Dewhurst contends that Hoel's framework supports weak or epistemic emergence, not ontological strong emergence. Macro-level gains in EI can be interpreted as better explanatory organization over the same underlying dynamics, rather than ontic novelty. Related objections include causal exclusion pressure and the fat-handedness problem for macro-interventions.

The paper's upshot is calibrated, not dismissive. Dewhurst concludes that the EI framework remains valuable for clarifying when coarse-grained models are epistemically superior and for studying inter-level explanatory autonomy. But he denies that the framework, without further assumptions, resolves classical metaphysical disputes in favor of strong emergence.

## Core Argument Map
- **Formal premise:** EI can increase under coarse-graining, so macro-level interventions can be more informative.
- **Causation question:** Informativeness under intervention does not automatically imply robust new causal powers.
- **Emergence question:** The demonstrated form of emergence is weak/epistemic, not strong/ontological.
- **Methodological pressure:** Fat-handedness and exclusion concerns limit metaphysical inference from EI alone.
- **Constructive result:** EI still offers a useful tool for analyzing explanatory scale-relations.
- **Conclusion:** Hoel-style causal emergence is philosophically important but metaphysically deflationary absent additional commitments.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure
This is a high-value interlocutor for RPNC because it formalizes the exact skeptical line that macro-level informational gains are only epistemic. RPNC's closure-based strategy can be framed as an explicit attempt to answer this challenge by adding admissibility discipline and transition-autonomy constraints before ontological commitment.

In reviewer terms, Dewhurst provides the most precise version of the objection that "better macro-model" does not entail "macro-objecthood." Engaging him directly can make the paper's argumentative structure clearer and show that the closure criterion is designed to block a known deflationary reading.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions
- This article appears in *Thought*, not in *European Journal for Philosophy of Science*.
- The paper is critical of metaphysical overreach, but not anti-formal or anti-macro by default.
- Its central distinction is inferential: explanatory gain versus ontological novelty.
- Any use in RPNC should avoid overstating Dewhurst as denying macro-level explanatory reality.
