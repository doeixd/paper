# Full Citation

Ladyman, James, and Don Ross, with David Spurrett and John Collier. 2007. *Every Thing Must Go: Metaphysics Naturalized*. Oxford: Oxford University Press.

# Detailed Summary

*Every Thing Must Go* advances a strongly naturalized metaphysics and argues that most contemporary analytic metaphysics should be abandoned as methodologically misguided. Ladyman and Ross call this target "neo-scholastic" metaphysics: projects that treat modal intuitions, conceptual puzzles about composition and identity, or armchair possibilities as if they could ground first-order ontology. Their positive alternative is to treat metaphysics as a unification project constrained by science rather than as a tribunal above science.

The core methodological proposal is the Principle of Naturalistic Closure (PNC). Roughly, a metaphysical claim is legitimate only if it helps unify specific scientific hypotheses, with at least one drawn from fundamental physics, and does explanatory work beyond what the separate hypotheses already provide. They also emphasize institutional filtering: what counts as serious input is not whatever can be imagined in philosophy, but hypotheses sustained by bona fide scientific practice. This is paired with the Primacy of Physics Constraint (PPC): special sciences should not violate settled physics, while physics is not reciprocally constrained by special-science theories in the same way. Importantly, this is weaker than standard ontological physicalism. The book rejects strong reductionist theses, rejects a strict hierarchy of "levels," and denies that all causation can be read as micro-level efficient causation.

The constructive metaphysical framework combines Ontic Structural Realism (OSR) and Rainforest Realism (RR), yielding Information-Theoretic Structural Realism (ITSR). OSR carries the claim that structure is ontologically primary: mature physics supports commitment to relational structure rather than to self-subsistent objects with intrinsic essences. RR then addresses the special sciences by defending a comparatively "lush" ontology of real patterns at multiple scales. Here they develop and revise Dennett's real-patterns idea: patterns are objective when they are projectible and non-redundant, not merely convenient for local predictive interests.

In this setting, the usual realism versus empiricism opposition is reworked. They accept key realist claims about mind-independent structure and scientific progress, but also adopt a verificationist attitude about epistemic value. Their verificationism is explicitly not a meaning theory. They argue for a deflationary, world-bound modality: laws and modal claims are about constraints in this measured/measurable universe, not about access to unconstrained metaphysical possibility spaces.

The account of causation is similarly revisionary. In fundamental physics, they resist treating causation as the universe's metaphysical "glue" and reject micro-banging pictures inherited from common sense. In special sciences, causal concepts are retained as indispensable heuristics for locating and stabilizing real patterns under local asymmetries and limited measurement. This supports explanatory autonomy without committing to mysterious downward causation or to anti-physicalist dualism.

The final chapters clarify that their position differs both from Dennett's illata/abstracta split and from Kantian phenomenalism. Against Dennett, special-science patterns are not merely approximate, human-purpose-dependent abstractions from a privileged base ontology. Against Kant, fundamental structure is empirically discoverable and revisable, not imposed a priori by fixed cognitive forms. Their naturalism is institutional and fallibilist: metaphysics should track the best current science, remain open to revision, and stop generating ontological commitments that science does not motivate.

# Core Argument Map

1. Contemporary neo-scholastic metaphysics is epistemically unproductive because it is weakly constrained by science.
2. Metaphysics should be reconstrued as scientific-worldview unification, governed by the PNC.
3. The PPC gives physics methodological primacy without entailing strong reductionist physicalism.
4. Structural realism is favored by theory change and modern physics, so ontology should prioritize structures over self-subsistent individuals.
5. Special sciences can still be ontologically serious: they track real patterns with restricted scope and scale.
6. Causation is not universal metaphysical glue; in special sciences it functions mainly as a heuristic for tracking asymmetric information flow.
7. Combining OSR and RR yields ITSR, which aims to explain realism, scientific practice, and unity without reviving armchair metaphysics.
8. Therefore, a defensible metaphysics is naturalized, science-led, fallibilist, and structurally realist rather than object-essentialist.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

This book is a major upstream source for a closure-based real-patterns project. It supplies the anti-gerrymandering orientation, the demand for scientific admissibility constraints, and the key idea that special-science ontology should be earned through objective, projectible pattern-tracking rather than through folk object metaphysics. It is especially useful where the closure project distinguishes strong ontological commitment from mere predictive convenience. At the same time, *Every Thing Must Go* does not by itself give a full closure protocol. Its real-patterns framework is powerful but comparatively permissive about sufficiency conditions and intervention-sensitive autonomy tests. So the relation is continuity plus tightening: the closure framework can be presented as a refinement of ETMG's naturalized and structural-realist program, not as a departure from it.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Do not report the book as denying all metaphysics; it rejects neo-scholastic metaphysics while defending a positive naturalized metaphysics.
- Do not collapse PPC into strong ontological physicalism; the text explicitly treats PPC as weaker and compatible with non-reductive special-science realism.
- Do not attribute eliminativism about special sciences to the authors; they defend reality of special-science patterns under disciplined constraints.
- Do not overstate their modality claims as anti-modal; they defend a deflationary, empirically answerable notion of modal structure.
- Do not say ETMG already provides the later closure criterion in full; it motivates and prepares that move but does not finish it.
