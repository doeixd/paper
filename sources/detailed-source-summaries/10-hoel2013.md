# hoel2013

## Full Citation
Erik P. Hoel, Larissa Albantakis, and Giulio Tononi, "Quantifying causal emergence shows that macro can beat micro," *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 110, no. 49 (2013): 19790-19795. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1314922110.

## Detailed Summary
Hoel, Albantakis, and Tononi argue that debates about emergence and reduction are often blocked by an inadequate causal metric. Their central move is to define and apply *effective information* (EI) as a state-independent measure of causal power, then compare EI across levels that are explicitly supervenient on a fixed micro-model. The paper aims to show that one can accept supervenience and still find cases where a macro description is causally superior to the micro description.

The framework begins from perturbational causal modeling: the system is set into all possible states with equal probability (counterfactual interventions), and one tracks resulting state transitions. State-dependent measures are introduced first, via cause and effect information (KL divergence between constrained and unconstrained repertoires). The authors then define EI as the average of these measures across states. In time-invariant systems, EI can be expressed as expected cause information, expected effect information, and mutual information between unconstrained causes and unconstrained effects. Conceptually, EI increases when mechanisms are selective and reliable, and decreases when transitions are noisy (indeterministic divergence) or degenerate (many distinct prior states converging on the same outcome).

Critically, EI depends on two factors that can move in opposite directions under coarse-graining: (i) repertoire size (number of possible states), and (ii) effectiveness (roughly determinism minus degeneracy). Macro models usually lose repertoire size, which pushes EI downward. But macro models can also increase effectiveness by averaging away noise or reducing degeneracy, which pushes EI upward. The authors define causal emergence as positive difference between macro and micro EI: $CE = EI(S_M) - EI(S_m)$.

The results are demonstrated in simulated binary systems. In the spatial case, grouping noisy micro AND-gate elements into macro elements yields higher EI because macro transitions become more deterministic and less degenerate, and the effectiveness gain outweighs the state-space loss. A second spatial example shows emergence mainly through reducing degeneracy rather than noise. In the temporal case, coarse-graining over longer time windows captures higher-order Markov dependencies that are largely invisible at a single micro step; EI rises accordingly. In the spatiotemporal example, both grouping in space and grouping in time raise EI substantially in a neural-like network with heterogeneous activity and connectivity, again because improved effectiveness dominates size reduction.

The discussion emphasizes constraints meant to keep the micro-macro comparison fair: no extra macro-only laws, mappings that discard micro identities, and macro interventions built by averaging relevant micro interventions so macro alternatives are tested uniformly. Within those constraints, the authors claim causal exclusion does not force reductionism. If causation is counted at one level at a time and macro EI is higher, macro should be treated as the privileged causal level. They call this "supersedence": macro supervenes on micro but can supersede it causally.

The paper also acknowledges practical limits. Exhaustive EI search over all coarse-grainings is intractable for realistic complex systems. So the authors offer heuristic indicators: emergence is unlikely when micro effectiveness is already high, and more likely when coarse-graining strongly raises effectiveness relative to the unavoidable size penalty.

## Core Argument Map
- **Problem setup:** Standard supervenience plus causal exclusion arguments are often interpreted as implying micro-level causal completeness.
- **Methodological claim:** This implication is not settled unless causation is measured with a metric sensitive to both mechanism quality and state-space structure.
- **Metric proposal:** EI captures average causal informativeness under intervention, with effectiveness decomposable into determinism and degeneracy.
- **Comparative test:** Evaluate EI at micro and admissible macro levels generated by explicit coarse-grainings that preserve supervenience.
- **Main result:** Some systems have higher EI at macro scales because increased effectiveness can exceed losses from reduced repertoire size.
- **Conclusion:** In those systems, genuine causal emergence occurs, so macro descriptions are not merely convenient summaries but the causally optimal model.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure
This source is relevant because it gives a formal route from "better macro model" to "causally superior macro level" without denying microphysical supervenience. For a closure-based realism project, the key contribution is the explicit tradeoff structure: a macro level earns priority only if it improves causal effectiveness enough to compensate for coarser state space.

At the same time, this paper does not by itself establish ontological realism about all macro patterns. Its demonstrations are in toy systems with exhaustive intervention access and designer coarse-grainings. So the strongest defensible use in *Real Patterns Need Closure* is methodological and conditional: Hoel et al. provide a principled test showing that some supervenient macro models can carry more causally informative structure than their micro bases, a result a closure account can integrate and then generalize with further constraints.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions
- The evidence is simulation-based, not direct confirmation in large natural systems.
- The framework presumes discrete state spaces and tractable transition modeling; real systems may violate these assumptions or only approximate them.
- "Macro beats micro" is conditional on selected coarse-grainings and intervention distributions, so robustness across alternative mappings is a key follow-up question.
- The paper argues for level-optimal causation within a system, not blanket anti-reductionism.
- Practical applicability is limited by combinatorial explosion in searching all coarse-grainings.
