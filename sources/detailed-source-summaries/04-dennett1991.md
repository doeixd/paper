# Full Citation

Dennett, Daniel C. 1991. "Real Patterns." *The Journal of Philosophy* 88 (1): 27-51.

# Detailed Summary

In "Real Patterns," Dennett argues for an intermediate position between strict realism and eliminativism about beliefs. He rejects the idea that intentional states must either be fully vindicated as brain-level entities or discarded as fictions. Instead, he proposes that beliefs are real insofar as they belong to genuine patterns that support successful prediction and explanation. His central strategy is to shift the ontology question from metaphysical absolutism to a scientific-pragmatic question: what counts as a real pattern in data?

Dennett introduces a model case with noisy dot arrays. A pattern is real, he suggests, when the data can be compressed, that is, described more efficiently than by a point-by-point bit map. This draws on algorithmic-information intuitions: a wholly random sequence is incompressible, while a patterned sequence permits shorter description. In this sense, the existence of a pattern is tied to objective structure in the data, not merely to an observer's arbitrary projection. At the same time, pattern detection is perspective-sensitive. Different organisms, tools, or theoretical interests can make different patterns salient. Dennett uses this tension to defend a "mild" realism: pattern reality is objective enough to constrain prediction, but not committed to a unique, perspective-free decomposition of the world.

He then develops this through examples of prediction under uncertainty. Competing pattern descriptions may differ in complexity and error tolerance. A simpler model with more tolerated noise may outperform a more detailed model in practice because it is computationally tractable and still predictively strong. Dennett treats this as a general feature of inquiry, including science, where idealizations are routinely accepted for practical and explanatory gain. So realism about patterns is inseparable from trade-offs among compression, tractability, and predictive success.

The Game of Life provides the bridge to intentional explanation. At the micro-level, Life is fully deterministic under a single update rule. Yet higher-level objects such as gliders, eaters, and even a simulated Turing machine become indispensable for understanding and prediction. Dennett uses this to motivate layered ontologies: higher-level entities can be real without being fundamental particles. Adopting a design or intentional stance can yield enormous predictive compression relative to brute micro-simulation. This is not because the higher-level talk is magical, but because it captures robust regularities that matter for forecasting behavior.

Applied to folk psychology, the point is that belief-desire explanation achieves major predictive leverage. Dennett grants noise and error, but argues that this does not undermine realism. Against Fodor, he denies that intentional regularities must correspond to cleanly tokened sentence-like structures in the brain to be real. Against Rorty, he denies that intentional success is merely conversational solidarity; predictive track record differentiates folk psychology from failed systems such as astrology. Against Churchland, he argues that eliminativism overreaches unless an alternative framework beats intentional explanation not only in principle but in practical, general-purpose predictive utility. Against Davidson, Dennett presses a stronger form of indeterminacy: two rival intentional interpretations can both be real patterns, both predictively competent overall, and yet disagree in specific cases without a deeper fact uniquely selecting one as "the" correct attribution.

Dennett's conclusion is deliberately modest but substantive. He does not claim to refute stronger realism or eliminativism outright. He claims those views are stronger than current evidence warrants, while mild realism about intentional patterns is already empirically and methodologically defensible. Beliefs are real as elements of projectible patterns whose explanatory and predictive power is demonstrable, even when the mapping between pattern and underlying mechanism is noisy, idealized, or multiply realizable.

# Core Argument Map

1. Ontological debates about belief are misframed by a forced binary between robust realism and elimination.
2. A better test of reality is pattern realism: a pattern is real when it supports nontrivial compression and successful prediction.
3. Pattern reality is objective but perspective-indexed, because detection depends on available capacities and interpretive stance.
4. Scientific and everyday inquiry both rely on pragmatic trade-offs among model simplicity, computational cost, and error tolerance.
5. The Game of Life shows that higher-level kinds can be real and explanatory despite microphysical completeness.
6. Folk-psychological interpretation is justified by predictive performance, not by proof of one-to-one neural sentence tokens.
7. Stable indeterminacy is possible: distinct intentional schemes can be equally real and useful without a deeper tie-breaker.
8. Therefore, mild realism about beliefs is the most defensible stance given actual explanatory practice.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

This essay supplies an important starting point for a closure-focused account of real patterns, but it does not itself deliver a full closure criterion. Dennett gives a strong compression and prediction-based test for pattern reality and shows why level-relative ontology can be legitimate. That helps motivate the claim that macro-level descriptions can earn ontic status when they provide robust leverage unavailable from brute micro-description. However, Dennett mainly emphasizes compressibility, pragmatic success, and stance-relative detectability, while leaving underdeveloped a stricter condition for when a pattern is causally self-sufficient across interventions. For a "real patterns need closure" framework, his contribution is thus foundational but incomplete: it authorizes intermediate realism and multi-level explanation, yet still needs supplementation by an explicit account of causal closure, interventional invariance, or autonomy thresholds that distinguish merely useful summaries from genuinely closed higher-level structures.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Dennett's criterion is not a simple verification rule; compression and prediction are presented as guiding marks of reality, not as an infallible algorithm.
- The paper does not claim intentional explanations are unrevisable, only that eliminative replacement requires a demonstrably better, tractable alternative.
- Indeterminacy claims are restricted to some cases and levels of idealization; Dennett explicitly allows that many concrete attribution hypotheses can be refuted.
- The argument is anti-binary rather than anti-realist: it defends a moderate realism about patterns, not pure instrumentalism.
- The Game of Life discussion is analogical and methodological, not direct empirical evidence about human cognition.
