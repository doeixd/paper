# Full Citation

Pawel Gladziejewski, "Real patterns, the predictive mind, and the cognitive construction of the manifest image," *Synthese* 206:225 (2025). Citation key: `gladziejewski2025`.

# Detailed Summary

Gladziejewski extends Dennett's real-patterns framework from a mainly metaphysical thesis about already-posited entities to a cognitive genealogy of how manifest-image ontology is formed. The paper's central move is to interpret predictive cognition itself as real-pattern extraction. Rather than treating beliefs, selves, or ordinary objects only as candidates for ontological vindication after the fact, Gladziejewski asks how cognitive systems come to represent such entities at all. His answer is that model-building minds compress sensory streams by tracking non-random regularities, and that this compression process is mathematically continuous with the ideas behind Dennett's notion of patternhood.

The technical arc runs from Kolmogorov-Chaitin complexity to computable proxies used in cognitive science. Because exact Kolmogorov complexity is uncomputable, the paper foregrounds Total Description Length (TDL) and the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. TDL balances model complexity and data fit; MDL selects models that minimize this combined cost. Gladziejewski then maps this compression logic onto predictive coding and Bayesian inference, arguing that selecting high-posterior models is equivalent, under standard coding assumptions, to selecting efficient encodings of data. The core claim is not merely analogical: inference and compression are presented as two aspects of the same computational objective.

This convergence culminates in predictive processing formulations that minimize variational free energy. On Gladziejewski's reading, free-energy minimization preserves the same complexity-accuracy tradeoff as MDL. Generative models compress sensory input by inferring hidden causes, while prediction-error minimization operationalizes ongoing model revision. The manifest image is therefore construed as the output of hierarchical, temporally structured pattern extraction. Perceived entities such as objects, selves, and social minds are treated as higher-level regularities that support efficient prediction.

From this cognitive reformulation, the paper develops a structuralist realism about the manifest image with an explicitly Kantian constraint. The manifest image can be veridical as a structural representation of patterns in sensory input, even if deeper noumenal realizers differ across scenarios (including skeptical scenarios like brains in vats). In this sense, the view is realist about structure at the phenomenal level, but modest about access to realizers, intrinsic non-structural properties, and deeper scientific structure not directly induced in ordinary perception.

Section 4 applies the framework to two debates. For the self, Gladziejewski argues that predictive systems infer a self-model as a common cause of endogenous sensory regularities across timescales. This supports a distinction between minimal and narrative self-modeling. The metaphysical upshot is explicitly bifurcated: anti-realism about substantial selves can coexist with realism about selves as patterns. For ordinary objects, the paper aligns with compression-based responses to the special composition question. Ordinary objects are privileged where grouping yields strong compression and predictive cohesion; arbitrary fusions are disfavored because they do not support efficient modeling. This is used to resist both nihilist and universalist revisionism by reconnecting metaphysics to perception understood as pattern-sensitive inference.

The conclusion emphasizes scope discipline: although the argument is developed via Bayesian and free-energy frameworks, the broader claim is intended to generalize to any plausible theory that treats cognition as information compression.

# Core Argument Map

1. Dennett's real-patterns account grounds pattern reality in objective compressibility facts, but mainly addresses already-articulated ontologies.
2. Cognitive science offers computable compression formalisms (TDL/MDL) that preserve the core patternist logic.
3. Predictive coding, Bayesian inference, and variational free-energy minimization can be read as implementing this compression-inference logic.
4. Therefore, manifest-image construction is a process of extracting real patterns from sensory input.
5. If manifest entities are structurally successful compressions, realism about the manifest image is warranted in a structural, phenomenally indexed sense.
6. Applications: selves are real as patterns (without requiring substantial selves), and ordinary objects are metaphysically privileged where compression and predictive integration are strongest.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

This paper is strongly complementary to a closure-centered development of real patterns, but it does not by itself supply a full closure criterion. Its main contribution is genealogical and cognitive: it explains how pattern-sensitive model construction could generate manifest ontology. For a closure-oriented project, Gladziejewski provides (i) a rigorous bridge from compression metrics to predictive cognition, (ii) a realism template that is objective but non-reductive, and (iii) a way to connect metaphysical debates about selves and objects to model-selection dynamics. At the same time, the paper remains mostly at the level of compression/inference success and does not fully articulate stronger autonomy tests (for example, explicit intervention robustness or formal closure/lumpability constraints). So it is best used as a high-value upstream foundation for closure arguments, not as their substitute.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- The argument depends on a representational-realist reading of predictive processing; Gladziejewski acknowledges instrumentalist and non-representational alternatives and concedes weaker uptake there.
- The structural-Kantian realism claim is carefully bounded to phenomenal structure and should not be overstated as access to noumenal realizers.
- The self discussion is framework-sensitive: conclusions differ depending on whether one requires substantial identity conditions or accepts deflated pattern realism.
- The ordinary-object application is presented as a promising sketch, not a completed solution to all composition puzzles.
- The paper explicitly brackets broader social-epistemic construction issues in favor of early, low-level perceptual modeling.
