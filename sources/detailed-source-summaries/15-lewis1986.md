## Full Citation

Lewis, David. *On the Plurality of Worlds*. Oxford: Blackwell, 1986.

## Detailed Summary

Lewis defends modal realism, understood as a literal ontological thesis: there are many worlds, not just one, and they are entities of the same basic kind as our world. Our world is "actual" only in an indexical sense, meaning this-worldly from our standpoint. Other worlds are not at any spatial or temporal distance from us, because worlds are spatiotemporally and causally isolated from one another. Lewis frames this as a metaphysical claim about what exists, not as a thesis about language alone, epistemic limits, or antirealist semantics.

The book's positive case is largely theoretical and comparative. Lewis argues that many philosophical analyses become more unified and economical if they quantify over worlds and possibilia. He develops this for modality (possibility and necessity), counterfactuals and comparative closeness, content of belief and knowledge (including de se structure), and properties and propositions (with properties treated as sets of instances across worlds). The recurring methodological claim is Quinean in spirit: a theory may rationally accept controversial ontology when doing so buys systematic explanatory power.

Lewis then clarifies core tenets: worlds are maximal sums of worldmates; worldmates are unified by spatiotemporal relatedness; no overlap between worlds (with limited qualifications, for example possible universals); and actuality is indexical rather than absolute. For plenitude, he rejects naive slogans like "every way a world could be is a way some world is" as potentially trivial unless properly regimented. He replaces them with a recombination principle, qualified by "size and shape permitting," to capture the thought that possibilities should not be arbitrarily sparse. He also insists that acceptable modal theory must allow alien possibilities, not only rearrangements of this-worldly kinds.

Chapter 2 answers objections. Lewis resists paradox arguments by rejecting auxiliary premises, especially unrestricted recombination principles that generate set-theoretic style contradictions. He addresses epistemic worries ("How can we know?") by arguing that modal and mathematical knowledge are not well modeled by simple causal-contact requirements. He further argues that modal realism does not force inductive skepticism, moral indifference, or a genuine explanation of brute arbitrariness in this world. On his view, plurality can reframe arbitrariness but does not eliminate the explanatory burden for this-worldly causal and nomological questions. He treats the "incredulous stare" as a serious but non-decisive cost tied to conservatism and common sense.

Chapter 3 evaluates ersatz alternatives. Lewis distinguishes linguistic, pictorial, and magical ersatzism, arguing they face different but serious difficulties. Linguistic ersatzism, though often the strongest competitor, risks conflation and reliance on primitive modality in the background. Pictorial ersatzism is meant to avoid language-dependence but still inherits modal circularity and representational problems. Magical ersatzism, in his presentation, relies on unexplained primitive representation relations or "selection" relations that he views as obscure. His dialectical upshot is not that all alternatives are impossible, but that each incurs substantial costs that can rival or exceed the ontological cost of genuine modal realism.

Chapter 4 separates confused from substantive "trans-world identity" questions. Lewis rejects overlap of worlds as a general solution because of the problem of accidental intrinsics, and he defends counterpart theory for de re modality. He allows trans-world individuals in a mereological sense if composition is unrestricted, but treats them as theoretical oddities rather than ordinary objects of discourse. He rejects haecceitism, in his defined sense of non-qualitative differences between qualitatively indiscernible worlds, and emphasizes the context sensitivity and occasional indeterminacy of de re modal claims.

## Core Argument Map

1. Philosophical analyses across multiple domains work better with possible-world resources.
2. The best literal interpretation of that practice is ontological: worlds and possibilia exist.
3. Modal realism is therefore supported by theoretical utility, unity, and economy, though not proven conclusively.
4. Major objections either depend on contestable auxiliary assumptions or impose comparable costs on rivals.
5. Ersatz substitutes are heterogeneous and each pays its own price; none straightforwardly dominates.
6. Counterpart theory plus non-overlap and indexical actuality gives Lewis his preferred package.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Lewis is relevant mainly at the methodological level. He repeatedly treats ontology as answerable to global theoretical virtues (unity, economy, explanatory integration), not just local intuitions. That is structurally consonant with a closure-oriented argument that evaluates frameworks by their system-level performance. At the same time, his case warns against overstatement: utility supports ontology defeasibly, and he explicitly declines to claim knockdown necessity. For "Real Patterns Need Closure," Lewis can support a disciplined "price-versus-payoff" inference from representational success to ontological commitment, while also modeling how to keep that inference fallible, comparative, and sensitive to alternative packages.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Lewis's defense is comparative and abductive, not a strict proof from neutral premises.
- Several key moves depend on contentious background commitments (for example recombination, unrestricted composition, and treatment of modality as quantification over worlds).
- His response to skepticism and ethics is largely to deny additional consequences, not to derive new positive ethics or epistemology.
- The treatment of ersatzism is version-sensitive; criticisms of one form do not automatically transfer to all forms.
