# Full Citation

Woodward, James. *Causation with a Human Face: Normative Theory and Descriptive Psychology*. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2021.

# Detailed Summary

Woodward argues that work on causation should be organized around sustained interaction between normative theory and descriptive psychology, rather than insulated projects. A central thesis is that causal reasoning is a human activity shaped by our goals (especially prediction, control, and generalization) and by epistemic limits, but this does not force subjectivism. He defends interventionism and minimalist realism: causal claims are objective insofar as they are answerable to how the world would respond under interventions, while expansive metaphysical additions are not required for his aims. On this view, there is no simple entailment from descriptive facts about how people judge to normative claims about how they should judge, yet there are structured links.

Across the book, Woodward treats standard philosophical dichotomies as often overused "dismissal" devices. He resists sharp separations between normative and descriptive inquiry, and between metaphysics and epistemology or methodology. His recurring claim is that a plausible account of causation should fit successful inferential practice: if causal claims are about intervention-supporting structures, then accepted methods (experiments and selected non-experimental designs) should make sense as ways of finding those structures.

The most developed constructive contributions concern distinctions within causation, especially invariance and proportionality. Invariance is treated as both a threshold and a graded notion. A relation must be invariant under at least some interventions to count as causal at all, but causal relations can then vary in how widely they remain operative across interventions, background conditions, and related changes. Woodward emphasizes that invariance is modal and regime-relative: most scientifically useful generalizations are effective within domains, not exceptionless everywhere. He also argues that invariance conditions guide inquiry architecturally, by structuring defaults, hypothesis search, and revision. Importantly, he denies that invariance is "evidence for truth" in an inference-to-best-explanation sense; rather, invariance identifies a class of truths that inquiry values.

Chapters on empirical psychology use this framework to interpret causal-learning results, especially work by Patricia Cheng and collaborators, plus Lombrozo and others. Woodward presents Cheng's causal power model as a case where invariance assumptions improve generalizability over simpler observable statistics such as $\Delta p$. He also reviews controversies and mixed findings, including critiques that not all subjects follow the same strategy. The empirical upshot is that multiple causal-strength probes track different dimensions (support or confidence, power-like judgments, background invariance, etc.), and better decomposition of these dimensions is methodologically needed.

In the final chapter, Woodward revises and tightens his earlier treatment of proportionality. He rejects the strong claim that only proportional causal claims are true. Instead, proportionality is a normative desideratum about variable choice when candidate cause variables stand in non-causal hierarchies of granularity (for a fixed effect variable). His proposed condition (P) says that better choices avoid both false dependency attributions and omission of dependency information that should be represented. This allows him to explain why overly specific or overly coarse causal descriptions can be deficient without treating them as straightforwardly false in every case.

# Core Argument Map

1. Causal theorizing is unavoidably both normative (how we should reason) and empirically constrained (how cognition actually works).
2. Interventionism plus minimalist realism gives an objective but non-inflationary account of what causal claims are about.
3. Successful methods of causal inference should be intelligible given that metaphysical picture, so epistemology and metaphysics must constrain one another.
4. Distinctions within causation are needed beyond minimal causation; invariance and proportionality explain why some true causal claims are more informative, generalizable, or explanatory.
5. Empirical studies of causal judgment are relevant because they can reveal computational patterns and suggest normatively defensible structures, though they do not by themselves settle normative questions.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Woodward's framework is directly useful for a closure-oriented real-patterns project in three ways. First, his invariance-centered account gives a principled criterion for why some higher-level representations are better for explanation and intervention: they track dependencies that remain operative across relevant changes. Second, his proportionality condition helps justify grain selection without defaulting to maximal micro-specificity, which aligns with closure arguments that seek the right level of representation rather than the most detailed one. Third, his link between normative adequacy and empirically successful reasoning supports a non-relativist but non-metaphysically-inflated realism about patterns.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Woodward does not claim descriptive data alone justify normative standards; his view requires additional assumptions and separate normative argument.
- He does not treat invariance or proportionality as strict necessary conditions for all causal truth; they often function as graded quality dimensions among true causal claims.
- His realism is explicitly minimalist. Using him to defend heavier metaphysical conclusions would overstate the text.
- The psychological evidence he surveys is mixed and probe-sensitive, so claims of unified folk causal theory should be qualified.
- Proportionality in his final formulation is restricted to specific variable-choice contexts (hierarchically related candidate causes, fixed effect), not a universal filter on all causal claims.
