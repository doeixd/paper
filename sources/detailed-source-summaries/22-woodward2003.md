# Full Citation

Woodward, James. *Making Things Happen: A Theory of Causal Explanation*. New York: Oxford University Press, 2003.

# Detailed Summary

James Woodward’s *Making Things Happen* develops an interventionist account of causation and a corresponding theory of causal explanation. The central idea is that causal claims are fundamentally claims about **difference-making under interventions**: roughly, if changing X (in the right way) would change Y, then X is a cause of Y. Woodward does not treat interventions as merely practical manipulations by human agents; rather, intervention is a formal device for expressing counterfactual dependence in a disciplined way. This lets him connect causation to scientific practice without reducing causation to subjective controllability.

The book is structured against two major alternatives: (1) regularity or Humean-style accounts that overemphasize mere association, and (2) purely mechanistic or process accounts that can underplay the explanatory role of counterfactual dependence. Woodward argues that scientific explanation is strongest when it answers “what-if-things-had-been-different?” questions. Explanations therefore gain force by showing how an outcome depends on specific variables across a range of hypothetical changes. In this framework, explanation is not just subsumption under laws; it is the mapping of stable dependency relations.

Formally and methodologically, Woodward draws heavily on structural-equation style thinking. Variables are related by equations or functional dependencies, and interventions are represented as exogenous changes that break ordinary incoming influences on the manipulated variable. This yields a clear way to separate genuine causal structure from confounding, spurious correlation, and mere evidential relevance. A key virtue of the framework is that it allows local, domain-specific causal knowledge even when strict universal laws are unavailable.

Another major contribution is Woodward’s notion of **invariance**. Good causal generalizations are those that remain stable under a suitable range of interventions. Invariance comes in degrees; some generalizations are fragile and hold only in narrow conditions, while others are robust across many backgrounds and manipulations. Explanatory depth, on this picture, depends partly on the breadth and clarity of this invariance profile. This helps explain why some causal models are scientifically useful despite idealization: they capture dependencies that are stable enough for prediction, control, and understanding.

Woodward also addresses problems of causal direction and asymmetry. Interventions help distinguish cause from effect because idealized interventions on effects do not typically propagate “backward” to causes in the same way. He similarly handles issues of omission, probabilistic causation, and causal relevance by asking whether proposed causes would make a systematic difference under appropriately specified manipulations. The account is thus flexible enough for biological, social, and special-science contexts where deterministic micro-laws are not practically central.

In relation to explanation, Woodward resists the idea that explanation must always be reductive. Higher-level variables can be explanatory when they support stable interventionist dependencies, even if lower-level realizers differ. This is especially important in complex systems: explanatory success often depends on selecting variables that are coarse-grained enough to support robust regularities yet fine-grained enough to track actionable dependence.

Overall, *Making Things Happen* is influential because it unifies causation, explanation, and scientific practice around a single criterion: intervention-guided counterfactual difference-making. It provides a rigorous yet pluralistically usable framework for evaluating explanatory claims in both experimental and non-experimental sciences.

# Core Argument Map

1. Scientific explanation is centrally about answering counterfactual dependence questions.
2. Causal claims are true when changes in putative causes would change effects under suitable interventions.
3. Structural equations provide a clear representational language for such dependencies.
4. The quality of causal/explanatory generalizations is measured by invariance across interventions.
5. Explanatory depth increases with robustness, scope, and precision of intervention-sensitive dependencies.
6. Higher-level explanations are legitimate when their variables track stable difference-making relations.
7. Therefore, causal explanation is neither mere law subsumption nor mere mechanism description, but interventionist mapping of dependable dependencies.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

For a project framed by “Real Patterns Need Closure,” Woodward is highly relevant because he offers a principled way to assess when a pattern is explanatory rather than merely descriptive. A “real pattern” on interventionist grounds is one that supports reliable counterfactuals: if one could intervene on pattern-relevant variables, downstream behavior would change in systematic ways. This links pattern reality to manipulable (or intervention-representable) dependency rather than to metaphysical sparsity alone.

The “need closure” aspect can be interpreted as requiring that explanatory patterns sit in a sufficiently closed dependency structure for inference and control. Woodward’s invariance criterion supplies exactly this closure test: a pattern is explanatorily closed enough when its governing generalizations remain stable under a relevant range of background changes and interventions. Fragile patterns with no stable intervention profile fail closure, even if statistically salient.

Woodward also helps with multi-level closure debates. If macro-patterns retain invariant dependency relations despite micro-realizer heterogeneity, they may satisfy closure at their own level. This supports a non-eliminative view in which closure is level-relative and model-relative, not automatically reserved for microphysics. At the same time, interventionism constrains permissiveness: not every coarse pattern qualifies, only those embedded in stable, testable counterfactual structure.
