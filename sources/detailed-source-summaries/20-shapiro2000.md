# Full Citation

Lawrence A. Shapiro, "Multiple Realizations," *The Journal of Philosophy* 97, no. 12 (2000): 635-654.

# Detailed Summary

In "Multiple Realizations," Shapiro argues that the standard philosophical use of multiple realization is often methodologically loose. He targets a common anti-reductionist inference: identify a higher-level kind, note different physical implementations, and conclude that type-level reduction is blocked. Shapiro does not deny physical heterogeneity. His narrower claim is that many famous examples do not establish the right kind of heterogeneity for strong conclusions about irreducibility.

The central move is to distinguish mere variation from realization-relevant difference. The issue is not whether systems differ physically at all, but whether they differ in properties that causally matter for the capacity in question. If differences are not explanatorily relevant, they do not support a robust multiple-realization claim. Shapiro therefore urges a stricter comparison of the lower-level features that do the work, rather than relying on coarse contrasts such as "biological" versus "artificial."

He develops this through engineering and biological illustrations often used in philosophy of mind. The key lesson is that coarse-grained difference can coexist with mechanistic similarity, and vice versa. His complaint is that philosophers often switch levels opportunistically: they treat broad physical difference as enough for multiple realization, while also treating broad functional similarity as enough for kind identity. That asymmetry, he argues, weakens the argument.

The paper also reframes the psychology-neuroscience relation. The familiar anti-reductionist story says that multiply realized mental kinds cannot be type-identified in lower-level science. Shapiro replies that this depends on how kinds are individuated. If taxonomy tracks causally relevant structure, some putative kinds may split, while others may permit local reductions. The result is not an all-or-nothing verdict on reduction but a case-by-case assessment tied to explanatory goals.

Shapiro’s broader point is methodological: metaphysical conclusions should not outrun empirical typing practices. He allows that special sciences classify by higher-level regularities, but rejects using that fact as a blanket argument against integration across levels. On his reconstruction, multiple realization is an empirical, model-sensitive claim requiring careful comparison of mechanisms and explanatory aims.

For philosophy of mind, the paper supports a deflationary but productive stance. Reduction is neither guaranteed nor impossible in principle. Instead, one should expect a mixed landscape: some kinds resist type-identification, some fragment under better science, and some support principled cross-level mapping. Multiple realization becomes a diagnostic whose force depends on detailed scientific practice.

# Core Argument Map

1. Standard anti-reductionist arguments rely on multiple realization of higher-level kinds.
2. Many cited cases conflate any physical difference with realization-relevant difference.
3. Only differences in causally relevant lower-level properties count for robust multiple realization claims.
4. Kind individuation should be constrained by explanatory practice, not by pre-theoretical labels alone.
5. Therefore, multiple realization does not by itself establish a global anti-reductionist conclusion.
6. The defensible outcome is plural and local: some reductions succeed, some fail, and some kinds are reclassified.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Shapiro is directly useful for *Real Patterns Need Closure* because he sharpens what should count as a genuine level distinction. Your closure framework asks whether a macro-description preserves intervention-relevant and prediction-relevant structure across perturbations. His critique of loose multiple-realization arguments reinforces that evidential standard and warns against inferring autonomy from superficial implementation diversity.

At the same time, his view leaves room for your positive thesis. If a macro-pattern tracks causally relevant organization that recurs across substrates, that is exactly the kind of cross-instance regularity that can ground real-pattern claims. Shapiro blocks easy autonomy claims, but he does not force micro-level exclusivism. He pushes theorists to show robust, mechanism-sensitive commonalities, which aligns with closure-based realism.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- The paper is often read as anti-multiple-realization in general, but the more accurate reading is methodological tightening, not elimination.
- Shapiro’s case is strongest against coarse, slogan-level examples; it is less decisive against carefully specified mechanistic comparisons.
- The argument depends on judgments about which differences are causally relevant, and those judgments are partly theory-laden and domain-sensitive.
- If used in your manuscript, present it as a constraint on evidential standards, not as a knockdown argument for reductionism.
- Do not attribute to Shapiro claims that all psychological kinds reduce or that special-science autonomy is illusory.
