## Full Citation

Hilary Putnam, "Psychological Predicates," in *Art, Mind, and Religion*, ed. W. H. Capitan and D. D. Merrill (Pittsburgh: University of Pittsburgh Press, 1967). Reprinted as "The Nature of Mental States" in Hilary Putnam, *Mind, Language, and Reality: Philosophical Papers, vol. 2* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1975).

## Detailed Summary

Putnam frames the paper around a familiar mind-body identity question: whether pain is a brain state. He immediately resists two assumptions he takes to dominate mid-century analytic method: first, that identity claims are legitimate only if they follow from meanings, and second, that they are philosophically informative only when reductive. Against this, he treats identity questions as potentially empirical, by analogy with scientific identifications such as temperature with mean molecular kinetic energy. His key distinction is between concept identity and property identity. From the fact that someone can know they are in pain without knowing anything about neurophysiology, it follows only that the concepts differ, not that the states cannot be identical.

He then examines several objections to psychophysical identity talk. One objection claims pain-in-arm and brain-state talk cannot be identified because they are tied to different spatial locations. Putnam argues this spatial criterion is weak and ad hoc. Another objection says that all explanatory work can be done by saying pain is merely correlated with some brain state. Putnam replies that this confuses explanatory competition with logical equivalence.

The paper's main positive move is the functional-state hypothesis. Putnam proposes that pain is not, at least not plausibly, a specific physical-chemical state type of the brain. Instead, pain is a functional state of the whole organism. To make this precise, he borrows automata-theoretic machinery. Organisms are modeled as probabilistic automata with sensory inputs, motor outputs, and state-transitions governed by a machine table. A system can realize many distinct automata descriptions, so he distinguishes physical realization from "functional organization" under a description, and from "total state" relative to that description.

On this framework, being in pain is identified with being in a certain kind of total functional state, one characterized by its place in transition structure and by input channels that normally register bodily damage or dangerous conditions. The proposal is schematic rather than fully confirmed neuroscience. Putnam acknowledges that spelling out and confirming either a strict brain-state identity theory or his own functional alternative is, in detail, a large empirical project.

He presses a now-classic multiple-realizability challenge against type-identity theory: if pain were one physical-chemical brain state, that state would need to be shared across mammals, reptiles, mollusks, and any extraterrestrial life capable of pain, while excluding creatures without pain. He does not claim this is impossible, but calls it highly ambitious and less plausible than functional characterization across diverse realizations.

Putnam also distinguishes his view from behaviorism. He agrees that pain has behavioral significance, but argues that purely behavioral-dispositional analyses are either circular ("behave as if in pain") or false in relevant edge cases, such as organisms with damaged motor pathways where behavior is absent though pain-relevant internal conditions may differ. Functional characterization, by contrast, can classify such cases by internal organization and role, not by overt behavior alone.

## Core Argument Map

1. Identity statements need not be conceptual truths; some are empirical identifications.
2. Therefore, differences in meaning between "pain" and neurophysiological terms do not by themselves refute psychophysical identity claims.
3. Type-identity (pain = one physical-chemical brain-state type) is possible in principle but requires extreme cross-species and cross-possible-organism uniformity.
4. A more plausible hypothesis is functionalism: pain is a state-type defined by causal-functional role within organism-level organization.
5. Behaviorism fails because behavioral criteria are circular or extensionally inadequate in counterexamples.
6. Conclusion: the best philosophical working hypothesis is that pain is a functional state of an organism, likely multiply realizable by different physical substrates.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Putnam's argument supports a core closure-friendly idea: explanatory legitimacy can attach to higher-level state descriptions when those descriptions capture stable causal organization that is not tied to one microphysical realization. His distinction between physical realization and functional organization parallels the claim that macro-level patterning can be objectively real when it carries explanatory and predictive load across variable substrates. At the same time, this source does not provide a closure metric or a formal criterion for sufficient autonomy. It gives a conceptual defense of non-reductive level descriptions, not a completed theory of macro-causal closure. It is best used as precedent for multiple realizability and level-relative explanatory strength, while later closure work supplies stricter criteria.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- The scanned text includes OCR corruption and reversed/garbled segments; claims here are anchored in the legible portions and in well-known published formulations of this essay.
- Putnam presents a programmatic hypothesis, not a completed empirical demonstration; overreading him as having established functionalism conclusively would be inaccurate.
- He critiques specific forms of type-identity and behaviorism in context; the paper should not be treated as settling every later variant of physicalism.
- His automata formalism is schematic and mid-century; contemporary readers should avoid importing stronger computational theses than the text supports.
