# Full Citation

Peter van Inwagen, *Material Beings* (Ithaca, NY: Cornell University Press, 1990).

# Detailed Summary

In *Material Beings*, van Inwagen develops a systematic answer to the Special Composition Question: under what conditions do some things compose a further thing? His answer is radical but not nihilist. He rejects both unrestricted composition and ordinary artifact ontology, while affirming that at least some composites exist. The core thesis is that composition occurs when and only when the activity of some entities constitutes a life (plus trivial singleton cases). The main consequence is the "Denial": there are no tables, chairs, statues, stones, or other ordinary inanimate composites, but there are living organisms.

The early chapters set up the dialectical field by testing familiar candidates for composition, including contact and various bonding-based principles. Van Inwagen argues these proposals either overgenerate (producing implausible sums and spare entities) or fail to track what matters about persistence and unity. He treats these failures as evidence that purely spatial or merely local physical relations are not enough. Composition must be tied to a stronger organizing relation.

His proposed positive criterion is life. He characterizes a life as an individual biological event: a self-maintaining, homeodynamic process with unity and individuation. The living case is compared to storm-like or fountain-like persistence through material turnover. On this picture, an organism persists not by retaining the same matter but by sustaining a process that recruits, reorganizes, and releases matter.

A central argumentative move is linguistic and methodological. Van Inwagen repeatedly insists that denying tables does not force denial of ordinary truths said with "table" or "chair." He draws an analogy to Copernicus: ordinary speech can remain broadly true while metaphysical analysis revises literal ontology. To operationalize this, he offers paraphrase strategies that translate artifact talk into plural claims about simples arranged chairwise, tablewise, and so on. The aim is not synonymy but truth-preserving redescription at the level relevant to ontology.

He then applies the framework to stock puzzles: statue/lump cases, Ship of Theseus pressures, and related identity-through-change problems. His strategy is deflationary: many paradoxes arise only if artifacts are treated as genuine composite objects. If artifacts are virtual rather than real composites, many persistence puzzles lose their target.

The book also links composition to philosophy of mind and personal identity. Van Inwagen uses first-person existence and the unity of thought as pressure against strict nihilism, arguing that thinking is not plausibly a merely cooperative activity of many unrelated simples unless those simples compose a thinker. This supports at least organism-level composites. Later chapters test identity claims through brain-transplant and commissurotomy-style cases, generally resisting psychological-continuity shortcuts when they conflict with organismal continuity.

In the final sections, he confronts the Problem of the Many and vagueness. He argues that any serious "Moderate" answer to composition, including his own, must accept real vagueness in the world, not merely linguistic vagueness. He sketches formal machinery for borderline composition and borderline existence to block familiar logical objections.

# Core Argument Map

1. The Special Composition Question requires a principled criterion for when plurality yields one object.
2. Standard criteria (contact, simple bonding, unrestricted sums) are either arbitrary, overgenerating, or misaligned with persistence and unity.
3. Composition is best explained by a biological-organizational condition: the activity of some entities constitutes a life.
4. Therefore, genuine composites are organisms; ordinary inanimate artifacts are not additional composite objects.
5. Ordinary discourse about artifacts remains largely usable through paraphrase into claims about simples arranged artifact-wise.
6. This framework reduces many artifact-identity puzzles and supports organism-centered accounts of persistence, while requiring acceptance of objective vagueness at the margins.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Van Inwagen is a useful contrast case for *Real Patterns Need Closure*. He offers a strict, non-permissive answer to "what counts as one thing," which aligns with your anti-gerrymandering aim. His requirement that composition reflect an integrated, self-maintaining process is close to closure-style demands for non-accidental unity.

But his criterion is much narrower than your framework. He ties genuine composition to biological life, whereas closure arguments are intended to license robust non-biological macro-objects when they satisfy dynamical autonomy conditions. So he can be used as a foil: he is right that not every useful aggregate is an object, but arguably too restrictive in limiting objecthood to organisms.

Methodologically, his paraphrase program also supports your handling of ordinary language. It shows how one can preserve everyday truth-apt discourse while imposing stricter metaphysical standards.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Do not present van Inwagen as a mereological nihilist; he explicitly affirms composites in the organismal case.
- Do not attribute a fully worked biological criterion from contemporary science; his "life" account is partly programmatic and analogical.
- Treat the Copernican/ordinary-language analogy as a compatibility thesis, not a proof that all ordinary ontology is dispensable.
- His anti-artifact conclusions depend on broader commitments about composition, identity, and paraphrase; avoid lifting isolated claims out of that package.
- If used against permissive real-pattern views, note that his restriction to organisms is stronger than needed for most closure-based realism.
