# Full Citation

Kim, Jaegwon. *Physicalism, or Something Near Enough*. Princeton Monographs in Philosophy. Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press, 2005.

# Detailed Summary

Kim’s book asks what kind of physicalism can survive the main pressures in late twentieth-century philosophy of mind. The framing claim is that two issues, mental causation and consciousness, form the core “world-knot” for contemporary physicalism. Mental causation asks how mental states can make a causal difference in a physically structured world. Consciousness asks how qualitative experience can exist, and be intelligible, if the world is fundamentally physical.

The central argumentative engine is the supervenience/exclusion line. Kim treats mind-body supervenience as a minimal physicalist commitment and combines it with causal closure of the physical domain and an exclusion principle. The resulting pressure is that if a mental event is causally efficacious with respect to a physical effect, and that effect already has a sufficient physical cause, then the mental cause is either excluded or must be reduced in a way that avoids problematic causal competition. Kim presents this as a challenge to nonreductive physicalism: the position promises autonomy for the mental while preserving physicalism, but it struggles to secure robust mental causation on its own terms.

Chapter 3 extends this pressure against substance dualism by a causal argument: immaterial minds, as nonspatial entities, cannot be coherently integrated into ordinary causal relations with material events (or with each other) in a way that preserves explanatory utility. This is used to motivate ontological physicalism about what exists, even while leaving open hard questions about mental properties.

Kim then distinguishes reduction models. He is skeptical that Nagelian bridge-law reduction yields genuine reductive explanation in the mind-body case. He also argues that the explanatory arguments for psychoneural type identities do not establish those identities. In his treatment, identities can function as rewrite rules inside explanations, but do not by themselves generate explanatory force. So he does not think type-identity physicalism is secured by the explanatory strategies on offer.

The remaining positive option is functional reduction. For many intentional/cognitive states, Kim argues that partial functionalization is enough to begin real reductive work: identify causal role specifications, then locate physical realizers (often locally and multiply realizable across populations). This is intended to vindicate causal efficacy for cognition and agency without requiring a single global reduction schema.

Qualia are the decisive limit case. Kim argues that intrinsic qualitative character is not captured by causal role, invoking the plausibility of qualia inversion to support nonfunctionalizability. If so, intrinsic qualia are not physically reducible and remain outside causal integration with the physical domain, yielding a residual epiphenomenalism. He still proposes that some relational features of experience (notably similarities and differences among qualia) may be behaviorally accessible and functionally tractable. The final position is therefore mixed: ontological physicalism plus conditional reductionism, with successful reduction for intentional/cognitive mentality, but only partial accommodation of consciousness. Hence the book’s concluding formula: physicalism is not the whole truth, but “near enough.”

# Core Argument Map

1. Minimal physicalism includes mind-body supervenience, and physical inquiry assumes closure of the physical domain.
2. If mental causes are distinct from sufficient physical causes for the same physical effects, exclusion pressure arises (or one accepts implausible overdetermination).
3. Therefore nonreductive physicalism faces an instability: it aims to preserve mental distinctness and causal efficacy together, but cannot straightforwardly secure both.
4. Substance dualism does not solve this, because immaterial minds are argued to be causally/explanatorily idle in relation to the spatial physical world.
5. Type-identity and bridge-law strategies are not established by the explanatory arguments Kim reviews; functional reduction remains the most viable reductive route.
6. Intentional/cognitive properties are plausible candidates for functional reduction, while intrinsic qualia are not; result: agency/cognition largely saved, intrinsic qualia left as mental residue.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Kim is highly relevant to a closure-centered realism project because his exclusion framework ties ontological seriousness to causal integration. If a putative level or property class cannot be integrated into causal/explanatory structure without competition or redundancy, its realist standing is threatened. This meshes with a closure criterion that treats successful causal organization, rather than descriptive convenience, as the key test for robust pattern reality.

At the same time, Kim’s treatment of functional reduction supports a selective, non-global strategy: some higher-level kinds can be retained as causally real when their roles are stably realizable in physical mechanisms. This is congenial to a real-patterns view that expects disciplined, local vindication rather than one-shot reduction.

His handling of qualia also supplies a caution: one can maintain strong closure demands for causal efficacy while conceding a constrained residue that is not fully integrated. So Kim can motivate closure requirements for causal claims without forcing an all-or-nothing metaphysical victory.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- The book’s positive endpoint is conditional and partitioned, not triumphant global reductionism.
- Kim often treats claims as dialectical pressure points, not knockdown proofs.
- The argument for qualia’s irreducibility relies on modal and conceptual considerations (for example, inversion plausibility), which critics may contest.
- Claims about complete vindication of mental causation would overstate the text; Kim explicitly leaves a residual epiphenomenal issue for intrinsic qualia.
