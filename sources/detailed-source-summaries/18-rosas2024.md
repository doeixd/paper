# Full Citation

Fernando E. Rosas et al., "Software in the natural world: A computational approach to hierarchical emergence," arXiv:2402.09090 (2024).

# Detailed Summary

Rosas et al. argue that existing tools in complexity science can detect macro-level structure, but they do not adequately explain how macro-level dynamics are implemented by micro-level processes. Their proposal is to treat emergence through an analogy with software. Software is physically realized in hardware, yet its operative logic is partly independent of hardware details. The paper develops this as a formal account of when a macroscopic description is genuinely self-contained.

To do this, the authors work within computational mechanics and distinguish two predictive constructions. First, the $\epsilon$-machine of a process gives a minimal optimal predictor of that process from its own past. Second, they introduce a $\upsilon$-machine tailored to a coarse-grained target: it captures which distinctions in microscopic histories are necessary for predicting the future of a macroscopic variable. This lets them test whether crucial predictive distinctions remain only at the micro-level.

On that basis, the paper characterizes three closure conditions. Informational closure says microscopic histories add no predictive information about macro-futures beyond macro-histories. Causal closure says macro-level organization is sufficient for macro-level intervention and control. Computational closure says macro-level computational organization is a coarse-grained form of micro-level organization. The key theoretical result is that informational and causal closure are equivalent in the setting studied. The paper then proves that, for spatial coarse-grainings, causal closure implies computational closure. But the converse fails in general. This matters because it blocks a common overreach: not every computationally efficient macro-description has full autonomous causal standing.

The framework is linked to Markov-chain lumpability. In particular, strong lumpability is presented as a sufficient condition for causal closure. This bridges abstract closure criteria with established stochastic process theory and gives tractable conditions for checking closure in concrete models.

Another major contribution is structural. The authors describe a hierarchy of coarse-grained processes as a lattice-like organization of nested computational machines. They claim this hierarchy is best understood in theory space, in terms of relations among predictive machines rather than only state-space partitions. Interpreted this way, the hierarchy functions as a blueprint of functional architecture: each level corresponds to an autonomous subset of system computations.

The case studies are used to demonstrate scope across model classes. In Rule 150 cellular automata, parity behaves as a non-trivial computationally closed quantity. In the Ehrenfest model, strong lumpability supports causal closure for particle-count macrostates. In Glauber-Ising dynamics, total energy is treated as causally and computationally closed under symmetry assumptions. In random walks on community-structured networks, the framework identifies nested levels of closure. In agent-based migration-style models, aggregate location vectors can support closure under lumpability conditions. In Hopfield-type memory systems, projections onto stored patterns illustrate informational and computational closure and are used to discuss multiple realizability.

The paper closes by emphasizing three payoffs: efficient prediction, macro-level controllability, and a formal route for discussing substrate independence and multiple realizability. The authors also qualify that macro-processes remain constrained by micro-causal organization, so the view is not unconstrained substrate independence. They connect the framework to Marr-style level distinctions and note possible links with integrated-information approaches, presenting these as future work.

# Core Argument Map

1. Emergence research needs criteria for how macro-level dynamics are implemented, not only whether macro-patterns are detectable.
2. The software analogy motivates a closure-based test: a macro-process is robust when micro-details do not add intervention-relevant predictive content.
3. The $\epsilon$-machine and $\upsilon$-machine distinction formalizes this test by separating self-prediction from micro-to-macro prediction.
4. Informational closure and causal closure are equivalent in the paper's framework, making predictive and interventional autonomy tightly linked.
5. Causal closure implies computational closure for the target class of coarse-grainings, but computational closure alone is insufficient.
6. Therefore, closure relations define a principled hierarchy of autonomous computational levels, with lumpability supplying practical sufficient conditions in important stochastic systems.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

This source is directly useful because it supplies formal machinery for a central claim in *Real Patterns Need Closure*: macro-level reality claims should track closure properties, not mere compression or convenience. Rosas et al. distinguish levels that are only computationally economical from levels that are also interventionally autonomous. That distinction can strengthen arguments against naive reductionism and permissive pattern realism. It also supports a non-arbitrary hierarchy because closure claims are tied to machine-theoretic and stochastic criteria. The most defensible uptake is conditional and scoped: use the framework to identify when macro-level descriptions inherit autonomous predictive and causal authority, without drawing stronger metaphysical conclusions than the formal results license.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- The source text emphasizes formal results but reports them at a high level; theorem details, assumptions, and proof domains should be checked in the arXiv paper before relying on fine-grained claims.
- Several implications are one-way (for example, causal to computational closure in the specified setting); reviewers may object if these are presented as biconditionals.
- Lumpability is a sufficient route to closure in the discussion, not a universal criterion for all emergence claims.
- Case studies illustrate breadth, but each depends on model-specific structure (symmetries, dynamics, chosen coarse-graining), so generalization requires care.
- The substrate-independence analogy is explicitly qualified by micro-level constraints; overreading it as unrestricted implementation-independence would misstate the paper.
