# Full Citation

Ando, Albert, and Herbert A. Simon. 1961. "Aggregation of Variables in Dynamic Systems." *Econometrica* 29 (2): 111-138.

# Detailed Summary

Ando and Simon ask when aggregation in dynamic economic systems is defensible as more than convenience. They frame the problem in linear form: given a high-dimensional system $x(t+1)=x(t)P$, can one derive a lower-dimensional system for aggregate variables that still tracks the behavior relevant for explanation and policy? They treat this as an approximation problem, not an exact identity problem, and they explicitly emphasize that practical adequacy depends on use and decision context.

Their central proposal is the class of nearly decomposable systems. A matrix is completely decomposable when it can be permuted into block-diagonal form, so each block evolves independently. A matrix is nearly decomposable when $P=P^*+\varepsilon C$, where $P^*$ is block-diagonal and off-block effects enter through a small perturbation term. The substantive interpretation is straightforward: within-group interactions are strong, across-group interactions are weak but not zero.

The paper's main contribution is to show that this structure yields a characteristic temporal pattern. In the short run, each block behaves almost as though isolated. Weak links exist, but their cumulative effect remains small, so one can study subsystems with little error. In a middle period, intra-block adjustments have mostly settled, and variables inside each block begin to move in approximately fixed proportion. At that stage, the Lange-Hicks intuition that variables "move together" becomes approximately true as a consequence of system structure, rather than a prior assumption. In the long run, the full system's dominant root governs behavior, and cross-block influences are no longer negligible.

This staged result is the paper's most important methodological claim. Aggregation is horizon-sensitive. It is neither globally valid nor globally invalid; its adequacy depends on where the system sits in its dynamic trajectory. The authors use a heat-diffusion example to make this intuitive: temperatures equilibrate quickly within rooms and only later across rooms, so room-level measurement can be sufficient for slower dynamics even if office-level detail matters early.

Sections IV and V formalize the argument. For the distinct-root case, they use similarity transformations and perturbation continuity of roots and eigenstructures to prove bounded closeness between the nearly decomposable and decomposable systems for small $\varepsilon$. They decompose trajectories into terms with different rates of decay and show how dominance shifts across time intervals. For stochastic matrices, they provide an alternative theorem set using idempotent expansions. This version is less general but gives clearer visibility into how block-level transition dynamics emerge.

The numerical section illustrates both strengths and limits. Their aggregation-based inversion method for nearly decomposable matrices is compared with perturbation inversion. In their example, perturbation gives tighter elementwise approximation to the inverse, but aggregation better captures medium-run structural interpretation. They insist that approximation quality should be evaluated relative to decisions supported by the model, not only algebraic proximity to an identity product.

In conclusion, they extend the framework to multi-level hierarchies and connect it to debates on input-output aggregation, identification under approximate exclusion restrictions, and partial-dynamics arguments. Two cautions recur: even very weak links matter over long horizons, and empirical work should align the time unit of observation with the level of aggregation. Their overall thesis is therefore conditional but strong: where near decomposability holds, one can justify a principled transition from micro-dynamics to macro-aggregation without treating aggregates as arbitrary.

# Core Argument Map

1. **Problem setup:** Economists need aggregation, but exact aggregation conditions are too strict for most realistic systems.
2. **Structural proposal:** Model the coefficient matrix as a block-diagonal core plus small off-block couplings, $P=P^*+\varepsilon C$.
3. **Dynamic claim:** Small couplings imply separable timescales, with short-run quasi-independence of blocks and later aggregate-level co-movement.
4. **Aggregation result:** In the middle/long-run window, within-block proportional movement approximately satisfies the Lange-Hicks requirement.
5. **Methodological implication:** Aggregation is justified as a controlled approximation tied to structure, horizon, and purpose, not as a universal replacement of micro-models.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

For a closure-focused argument about real patterns, this source is useful because it supplies a concrete mechanism for when coarse-grained variables become dynamically adequate. Near decomposability explains how macro-level descriptions can be stable and informative even when micro-couplings remain physically present. Just as importantly, the paper constrains overreach: aggregation is justified only under structural and temporal conditions, and weak links eventually re-enter. So the text supports a disciplined realism about macro-patterns, where legitimacy comes from demonstrable regime-specific closure rather than mere convenience.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

The analysis is for linear systems under strong assumptions about eigenstructure and small coupling, so extension to nonlinear settings requires additional argument. The results are explicitly sufficient, not necessary, conditions for aggregation, so they do not provide a complete taxonomy of when aggregation works. Their stochastic exposition trades some formal precision for interpretive clarity, with full technical detail delegated to appendices. The matrix-inversion example also underlines that better interpretability and tighter numerical fit are distinct criteria.
