# elgin2017

## Full Citation

Elgin, Catherine Z. *True Enough*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2017.

## Detailed Summary

In *True Enough*, Catherine Elgin argues that a strict truth-centered epistemology fails to capture how successful inquiry actually works, especially in science. Her target is veritism, the view that truth and truth-conduciveness are the sole fundamental epistemic standards. Elgin’s opening claim is that this framework cannot explain the epistemic legitimacy of models, idealizations, and thought experiments that scientists knowingly use despite their literal falsehood. If these tools are judged only by literal truth, their core role in inquiry becomes mysterious or downgraded to mere heuristic convenience.

Elgin’s alternative is that many falsehoods are epistemically productive. She calls these cases “felicitous falsehoods”: inaccurate representations that still contribute to understanding. The key relation is exemplification. A representation can instantiate and selectively highlight some features while downplaying others. When done well, this selective salience makes otherwise hidden structure visible. On her view, the epistemic contribution of a model is often not that it mirrors its target, but that it reveals significant relations, patterns, or dependencies by simplifying, distorting, or idealizing in disciplined ways.

This requires a shift from a knowledge-first framework to an understanding-first framework. Elgin treats objectual understanding as a holistic achievement: a relatively integrated, usable grasp of a topic that supports inference, question-framing, and further inquiry. She argues that disciplinary inquiry is not best represented as accumulation of independently justified atomic truths. Accounts in science are interwoven systems of factual, methodological, and normative commitments. Their components are often not assessable one by one in isolation. Because of this, she develops a procedural holism grounded in reflective equilibrium: an account is tenable when it is, in present circumstances, as reasonable as available alternatives and can be reflectively endorsed despite recognized fallibility.

Her treatment of epistemic normativity is similarly anti-veritist in structure. Rather than grounding epistemic legitimacy in reliability toward truth alone, she advances a responsibilist, deontological picture. Epistemic agents should accept commitments they can endorse as members of a “realm of epistemic ends,” an idealized community where standards are public, criticizable, and mutually accountable. This introduces a social and ethical dimension to inquiry, especially where trust, division of labor, and integrity are necessary for collective knowledge practices. On this account, objectivity is not a “view from nowhere.” It is achieved through procedures that block idiosyncrasy, bias, and arbitrary privilege, including impersonal and impartial modes of assessment and disciplined perspectival work.

Chapters 9 to 13 extend the exemplification framework across science and art. Elgin argues that laboratory experiments, thought experiments, literary fictions, and artworks can all generate understanding by making features salient that ordinary perception or literal description leaves obscure. Representation is not exhausted by denotation plus resemblance. Successful representation-as depends on how symbols are used to exemplify relevant features and support warranted projection back to targets. This supports her broader claim that art and science are not epistemically disjoint domains, even though their framing commitments differ.

The final chapter recasts fallibilism as an active epistemic stance rather than a weak concession. Elgin argues that taking error possibilities seriously can improve inquiry by identifying fault lines in current commitments, sharpening methods, and revealing where accounts are most vulnerable. Error is not merely epistemic failure; in mature inquiry it can function as a driver of refinement and deeper understanding. The payoff of fallibility appears most clearly once epistemology centers on understanding rather than infallible knowledge.

## Core Argument Map

1. Veritism overstates the role of literal truth and underdescribes actual scientific practice.
2. Science relies on nonliteral devices (models, idealizations, thought experiments) that are often knowingly false.
3. These devices are epistemically legitimate when they function as felicitous falsehoods via exemplification.
4. Understanding, not atomized propositional knowledge, is the central achievement in systematic inquiry.
5. Epistemic legitimacy is grounded in responsible, publicly criticizable norms within communities of inquiry.
6. Fallibility is epistemically productive because error analysis improves accounts and methods.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Elgin is directly useful for defending non-mirroring but still answerable representation. Her account supports the idea that abstractions can be objectively constrained even when they are not strictly true in every local detail. For a closure-oriented framework, this helps justify disciplined idealization and selective salience as legitimate epistemic moves rather than defeaters.

At the same time, Elgin imposes constraints that matter for this project. “True enough” is not a blanket license for convenient simplification. A model must exemplify features genuinely shared with targets and must support responsible projection under explicit purposes and standards. So the book supports closure-style realism only if closure claims remain accountable to fit, interpretive discipline, and revisability.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Elgin does not claim that truth is irrelevant; she denies that truth is the sole or overriding epistemic standard in all contexts.
- The argument is anti-reductionist about epistemic standards, not straightforwardly anti-realist about the world.
- “True enough” is context-sensitive, which can appear permissive unless its methodological constraints are made explicit.
- The science-art parallel is based on a shared symbolic mechanism (exemplification), not on erasing differences in standards across disciplines.
- The “realm of epistemic ends” is an idealized normative model, not a descriptive claim that actual institutions already satisfy these conditions.
