## Full Citation

Jaegwon Kim. *Mind in a Physical World: An Essay on the Mind-Body Problem and Mental Causation*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 1998.

## Detailed Summary

Kim presents the mind-body problem as a problem internal to physicalism rather than a replay of Cartesian substance dualism. His framing claim is that contemporary philosophy of mind has mostly accepted a physicalist backdrop, but has hoped to keep robust mental autonomy through supervenience, realization, and nonreductive physicalism. Across four lectures, he argues that this package is unstable if mental causation is taken seriously.

In chapter 1, Kim distinguishes several post-identity-theory strategies and argues that supervenience alone is not a theory of mind-body relation. Supervenience, on his view, states a covariation constraint, often read as dependence, but it does not by itself explain why mental properties track physical properties. He emphasizes that very different positions, including type physicalism, emergentism, and even epiphenomenalist views, can endorse supervenience. Because of this, supervenience is at best a minimal physicalist commitment, not a substantive solution. He then develops physical realizationism (physicalist functionalism): mental properties are second-order functional properties instantiated via first-order physical realizers. This framework, he claims, can explain why supervenience holds and why mental-physical correlations are not brute. It also motivates a model of reduction centered on functionalization and realization, rather than Nagelian bridge-law derivation.

In chapter 2, Kim introduces three mental-causation problems: anomalism, extrinsicness/content externalism, and causal exclusion. He treats exclusion as central. The core pressure is familiar: if a mental event causes a physical event, and every physical event with a cause has a sufficient physical cause, what is left for the mental qua mental to do? He formulates this through a supervenience argument: if supervenience fails, mental causation becomes unintelligible for physicalists because it breaches physical closure; if supervenience holds, mental causes appear preempted by their physical bases unless one can specify a non-mysterious dependence relation. His diagnosis of nonreductive physicalism is that property dualism plus causal closure generates recurrent exclusion pressure.

In chapter 3, Kim examines deflationary replies that prioritize explanatory practice over metaphysics or seek low-cost reconciliations (for example, level-talk that treats mental and neural causes as harmlessly coexisting descriptions). He argues these moves do not dissolve the exclusion structure. Two causal explanations of one effect still require an account of their relation; “different levels” is not yet that account. He also engages worries about second-order properties and argues that the causal powers of such properties are inherited from realizers. This reinforces the thought that functional properties do not add independent causal force beyond their realizers.

In chapter 4, Kim reworks reduction. He criticizes Nagel-style reduction for relying on bridge laws that neither secure explanatory depth nor ontological simplification. His alternative is functional reduction: first functionally characterize a target property, then identify realizers under relevant nomological and structural conditions. This yields “local” or structure-specific reductions and supports the claim that reduction can be explanatory even when realizers vary across species or worlds. He also argues for caution about treating multiply realized higher-level properties as robust disjunctive properties with unified causal profiles. The endpoint is stark: if all mental properties are functionalizable, causal efficacy is preserved but only by integrating mentality into the physical domain; if some (especially qualia) resist functionalization, then physicalism faces a choice among epiphenomenalism, eliminativism, or a stronger dualist departure.

## Core Argument Map

- The mental must be causally efficacious for agency, knowledge, and psychology as a science.
- Physicalists usually accept both physical causal closure and mind-body supervenience (at least as minimal physicalism).
- Supervenience alone does not explain dependence; it is compatible with rival metaphysical packages.
- Under closure plus supervenience, mental causation faces exclusion unless mental properties are related to physical bases in a way that secures non-competitive efficacy.
- Functional realization offers such a relation for functionalizable properties: causal powers are inherited from realizers, enabling explanatory reduction.
- Properties that resist functionalization (Kim’s main candidate is phenomenal character) remain the pressure point for nonreductive physicalism.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Kim is highly relevant to a closure-based realism project because he treats closure constraints as metaphysically load-bearing rather than optional background assumptions. His critique of “bare supervenience” parallels the thought that mere cross-level covariance is not enough; one needs an account of the dependence relation that explains why higher-level regularities hold. This supports a closure-oriented demand that macro-level legitimacy must be tied to principled causal organization, not just stable correlation. At the same time, Kim is more reduction-pressured than many real-pattern views: he is skeptical that multiply realized higher-level kinds automatically have unified causal standing. So the strategic takeaway is two-sided: his exclusion framework strengthens the demand for explicit closure criteria, but his treatment of higher-level kinds sets a high bar that any causal-autonomy account must answer directly.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- The text is argumentative and programmatic, not a neutral survey; summaries should keep Kim’s “pressure argument” structure visible.
- Kim’s positive proposals are partly conditional (especially around intentionality vs qualia), so avoid reading him as a blanket eliminativist.
- He allows nomological relativity in realization and reduction; do not recast his view as requiring global, exceptionless type identities.
- His claims about disjunctive/multiply realized properties are controversial in later literature; report them as his diagnostic constraints, not settled consensus.
