## Full Citation

Jerry A. Fodor, "Special Sciences (or: The Disunity of Science as a Working Hypothesis)," *Synthese* 28, no. 2 (1974): 97-115.

## Detailed Summary

Fodor’s central claim is that a common formulation of the unity of science is too strong. He separates two theses often conflated: all events studied by special sciences are physical events, and all special-science laws and kinds must reduce to physical laws and kinds. He accepts the first thesis, token physicalism, and rejects the second, classical reductivism.

He reconstructs the standard reduction model: bridge principles connect special-science predicates to physical predicates, and a physical law mirrors the special-science regularity. On a strong reading, bridges are lawful event-identities, not mere correlations. This secures the physicalist intuition that events under special-science laws are physical events. But it also imposes a stronger condition, namely lawful correspondence between special-science kinds and physical natural kinds.

That requirement is what he attacks. Many successful special-science generalizations, he argues, group physically heterogeneous cases. Economics is his main example: even if every monetary exchange has a physical description, the covering physical description is likely disjunctive and not a physical natural kind. At best one might get brute coextension, not a law.

He extends the point to psychology. Even if every psychological event is a neurological event, psychological kinds need not map lawfully onto neurological kinds. Multiple realization, including machine realization, makes type-to-type reduction doubtful. Psychology may cross-cut neuroscience taxonomically without threatening token physicalism.

Fodor then sketches a weaker unity thesis. Instead of requiring lawful bridges between special and physical kinds, he allows true bridge statements whose physical side may be disjunctive and non-natural-kind. What matters is that physical mechanisms explain conformity and failure for special-science generalizations, not that each special-science kind be type-identical with a physical kind. This also explains why special-science laws can be exception-tolerant while basic physical laws remain exceptionless.

The payoff is methodological and metaphysical. Special sciences remain legitimate because they track robust, counterfactual-supporting patterns at their own level. Physics can remain ontologically basic in the token sense without supplying the only useful taxonomy. Unity of science becomes unity of subject matter, not forced unification of vocabularies.

## Core Argument Map

1. Classical reductivism requires bridge laws linking special-science predicates to physical natural kinds and matching physical laws.
2. On an identity reading, this yields token physicalism, but it also requires lawful kind-level correspondence.
3. Economics and psychology suggest that many special-science kinds are physically heterogeneous and multiply realizable.
4. So token physicalism does not entail reductivism.
5. A weaker unity thesis keeps physical realization while allowing disjunctive, sometimes non-lawlike bridges and preserving special-science autonomy.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Fodor’s paper is directly useful for a real-patterns framework that treats higher-level kinds as objective when they support stable, projectible generalizations. It offers a classic rationale for why special-science robustness need not depend on matching physics-level natural kinds.

At the same time, his position is not anti-physicalist. He insists on token physicalism and the generality of physics, which aligns with closure-oriented views that require physical realization of higher-level patterns. The key distinction is between ontological dependence (events are physical) and taxonomic reduction (kinds are physical kinds).

His account of exceptions is also relevant: special-science regularities can remain explanatory despite heterogeneous micro-realization. That fits closure-based arguments where macro-level predictive compression and interventional stability persist across diverse realizers. The caution is that Fodor does not provide a formal closure metric, so this is conceptual support rather than a full technical model.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Fodor targets a strong reduction model, so contemporary novelty claims should be carefully scoped.
- The case is mainly abductive and example-driven, not a formal impossibility proof.
- His treatment of natural kinds is partly schematic and open to sharper reformulation.
- Use him chiefly for conceptual distinctions, not as standalone evidence for any specific closure formalism.
