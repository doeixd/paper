# Full Citation

Goodman, Nelson. *Fact, Fiction, and Forecast*. Orig. 1955; 4th ed. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1983.

# Detailed Summary

This text presents a unified inquiry into counterfactuals, dispositions, lawhood, and induction. Goodman's central strategy is to shift attention away from searching for metaphysical guarantees and toward clarifying the standards by which inferences are actually licensed. The opening chapter ("Predicament," from 1946) develops a negative diagnosis of counterfactual analysis: a counterfactual cannot be handled as a mere truth-functional conditional, and attempts to define its truth by adding "relevant conditions" quickly encounter technical failures. If one includes all true background statements, contradiction trivializes inference; if one narrows to some true set, one can still derive incompatible consequents unless further constraints are added. Goodman introduces cotenability to block illegitimate background assumptions, but then argues that cotenability itself appears to depend on counterfactual judgments, producing regress or circularity. The chapter therefore does not solve counterfactuals, but shows why existing recipes fail.

The same chapter links counterfactual truth to lawhood. True generalizations are not enough; accidental truths (for example, about the specific contents of a pocket) do not support counterfactuals in the way laws do. Goodman explores increasingly strict criteria for "lawlike" statements, including predictive use and independence from determining any particular instance. Yet he concludes that these criteria require a better account of confirmation and projectibility than available theories provide. The key result is methodological: the hard problems of counterfactuals and laws cannot be solved in isolation from induction.

In "Project" (1953 lectures), Goodman reopens the same family of problems through dispositions and possibilia. He treats dispositional predicates (such as "flexible") as classificatory predicates over actual things, not as names of occult entities. The problem is not to posit hidden powers but to explain, in terms of manifest predicates and lawful connections, when projection from observed to unobserved cases is warranted. Likewise, talk of "possible" entities is reconstructed as talk about actual entities under projected predicates. His provocative slogan in this context is that possible worlds do not stand outside actuality: what appear as alternatives are often alternative descriptions and projections within the actual world.

Chapter III formulates the famous "new riddle of induction." Goodman accepts that the traditional demand for a non-circular proof of induction is misconceived; justification works by reciprocal adjustment between rules and accepted inferences (the same broad structure he attributes to deduction). But once that old problem is "dissolved," a constructive problem remains: what distinguishes projectible from nonprojectible predicates and hypotheses? The "grue" construction demonstrates that syntactic form and simple instance-counting are insufficient. Before time *t*, all observed emeralds can confirm both "all emeralds are green" and "all emeralds are grue," yet these support incompatible future predictions. This shows that confirmation depends on the predicates involved, not just logical form.

Across the prefatory notes to later editions, Goodman clarifies several frequent misreadings. Projectibility can vary over time; "grue" is not simply banned everywhere; and no non-question-begging syntactic or semantic test cleanly separates "good" from "bad" predicates. The 1973 and 1983 notes report revisions to the projectibility rules and emphasize that the account is pragmatic, historical, and fallibilist rather than foundationalist. Putnam's foreword reinforces this interpretation: Goodman's view does not require innate predicate orderings, and its force lies in showing that inductive validity is not formal in the same sense as deductive validity.

# Core Argument Map

1. Counterfactuals require more than false antecedents and true/false consequents; they depend on lawful connection plus relevant background conditions.
2. Attempts to specify relevant conditions generate paradoxes unless one adds cotenability constraints, but cotenability appears to reintroduce counterfactual dependence.
3. Therefore counterfactual analysis depends on distinguishing laws from accidental truths.
4. Lawhood and confirmation depend on whether predicates and hypotheses are projectible.
5. The grue case shows that formal/syntactic confirmation schemas underdetermine projection.
6. Inductive justification is consequently a matter of systematizing practice (historically conditioned standards, including entrenchment), not deriving prediction from purely formal logic.

# Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Goodman is directly relevant to a closure-based realism program because he argues that successful projection cannot be read off syntax alone and must track stable classificatory practice. In a "real patterns need closure" frame, this can support the claim that admissible predicates are constrained by whether they participate in robust, recursively usable inferential regimes, not by superficial grammatical form. His discussion of lawlikeness, projectibility, and entrenchment also provides a caution: any closure criterion must explain how historically developed inferential habits can be norm-governed without collapsing into arbitrariness. Goodman does not offer a final closure metric in the extant text here, but he does supply the key negative result that a purely formal criterion will not do.

# Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- This source is largely diagnostic and programmatic; it identifies failure modes more than it delivers a completed theory.
- The text repeatedly warns that "grue" does not by itself prove skepticism about induction; it exposes underdescribed constraints in confirmation theory.
- Goodman's own proposals are explicitly revisable across editions, so claims about a fixed final rule should be avoided.
- Entangling Goodman with strong nativist conclusions is textually risky; Putnam's foreword explicitly rejects that reading.
- The conversion used here appears to include material through Chapter III and paratexts, but not the full Chapter IV body listed in the table of contents.
