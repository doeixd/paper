## Full Citation

James Woodward, "Methodology, Ontology, and Interventionism," *Synthese* 192(11):3577-3599 (2015).

## Detailed Summary

Woodward argues that interventionism is best understood as a methodological program for causal inquiry, not a metaphysical account of causation. He distinguishes two senses of ontology. "Ontology1" is the scientific task of identifying the entities, structures, and classifications useful in a domain. "Ontology2" is the analytic-metaphysical project centered on grounding, truth-makers, fundamentality, and reduction. His framing claim is that methodology is naturally intertwined with ontology1, but often only weakly connected to ontology2.

Methodology, for Woodward, is goal-relative and constrained by human epistemic limits. In causal inquiry, one key goal is finding dependencies exploitable for manipulation and control. From this perspective, interventionism is a proposal about how to clarify, evaluate, and test causal claims: associate them with hypothetical experiments and ask what would happen to effect variables under interventions on candidate causes.

His basic interventionist schema says that $X$ causes $Y$ if intervention on $X$ is possible and some intervention-induced variation in $X$ produces variation in $Y$. He also emphasizes "soft" interventions, not only hard arrow-breaking manipulations. Soft interventions can support causal inference when they introduce exogenous variation in $X$ uncorrelated with off-path causes of $Y$.

Woodward then addresses recurring criticisms. He grants that interventionism is not reductive and does not provide metaphysical truth-conditions in the favored ontological2 sense. But he denies that this makes it uninformative. Its value is methodological: it helps specify inferential targets, disambiguate underspecified claims, and evaluate evidential procedures.

On inferential targets, he notes a widespread practice in econometrics and related fields: non-experimental causal inference is judged by whether it recovers what an appropriate experiment would show. The hypothetical experiment is thus a benchmark for correctness, even when not actually performed.

On disambiguation, he shows that ordinary causal statements often hide multiple non-equivalent commitments. "Smoking five packs causes lung cancer" can encode different interventions, and these may differ in truth value. Likewise, "being a woman causes hiring discrimination" is unclear until one specifies whether the intervention concerns chromosomal sex, employer beliefs, or some other variable. Interventionist reconstruction forces explicit variable choice and contrast specification, making causal content more precise and testable.

On limits of inference, interventionist framing can reveal when a causal question is not identifiable from available data, or even not answerable in principle under a given setup. His school-start-age example shows how algebraic constraints among age, maturation, and time in school can block disentangling effects, so no method can recover the target from that data structure alone.

He also argues that interventionism bears directly on non-experimental methods such as instrumental variables. Under standard IV conditions, the instrument functions like a soft intervention on $X$ relative to $Y$, which explains why the estimator can track causal effect despite confounding that invalidates naive regression.

In Section 9, Woodward challenges the idea that causal methodology must be grounded by metaphysical truth-makers, especially fundamental physical laws. Even if such grounds exist, they are typically inaccessible and non-action-guiding for upper-level inquiry. Reliable science often succeeds by insulating inference from microphysical details, not by deriving results from them.

He closes by proposing a conciliatory reconfiguration: many ontology2 disputes can be recast as methodological questions. Instead of asking what causation "really" is in metaphysical terms, ask which representational choices improve clarity, stability, exportability, and inferential reliability. He illustrates this with issues about event individuation over time, contrastive causation, and double prevention.

## Core Argument Map

1. Methodology is assessed by means-end fit under human epistemic constraints.
2. Causal inquiry has control/manipulation-oriented aims, so causal claims should be linked to intervention outcomes.
3. Interventionism, so construed, is a methodological framework, not a reductive ontology2 theory.
4. This framework earns support by practical payoff: target specification, claim disambiguation, identifiability diagnosis, and method assessment (for example, IV).
5. Metaphysical truth-maker programs do not currently improve these methodological tasks and are often epistemically unusable.
6. Therefore, causal methodology is substantially autonomous from ontology2, though some ontology2 debates can be productively transformed into methodological form.

## Relation to Real Patterns Need Closure

Woodward’s paper offers a template for defending closure-based realism without inflating metaphysical commitments. It supports giving priority to methodological criteria that track reliable interventional performance, especially where microphysical grounding is unavailable or irrelevant to practice. For *Real Patterns Need Closure*, this helps frame closure claims as objective constraints on good causal representation, not as mere convenience, while preserving a distinction between methodological success and stronger claims about metaphysical fundamentality. His discussion of variable choice and contrast specification is useful for showing how higher-level patterns can be precise, testable, and action-guiding.

## Reviewer-Relevant Cautions

- Woodward does not claim interventionism is uniquely correct; he presents it as a fruitful framework among possible alternatives.
- He does not argue ontology2 is meaningless; his stronger point is limited methodological usefulness for many causal-inference problems.
- The anti-reductive conclusion is bounded: one can clarify causal content and evidential standards without metaphysical truth-conditions.
- Several arguments are practice-based and comparative, so they support methodological orientation more than a knockdown refutation of metaphysical projects.
- Use of his conclusions in closure debates should keep clear whether a claim is methodological, ontology1-level, or a stronger realist thesis.
